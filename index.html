The World at a Glance

Overview

The year is 2184. Humanity has spread far across the solar system, but the further you get from Earth, the more the weight of its demand recedes. Nowhere is this truer than the moons of Saturn - a constellation of small, isolated worlds pressed together by their economics and a growing contempt for the distant authority that claims to own them.

This is not a story of heroes. It follows ordinary people caught in vast forces: an empire's tightening grip, a colony's struggle for independence, and smaller stories unfolding in the void between. It’s about debt, and what happens when you become expendable.

Setting Tone

Claustrophobic. Utilitarian. And politically charged. The aesthetics draw from brutalist architecture and are inspired by analog technology - the quiet terror of knowing the atmosphere outside your walls would kill you in minutes.

Inspired by works like Orion Arm, SAVAGES, The Sojourn, SAD: Frontier, The Expanse, For all Mankind

Earth & the Solar System

The Larger Picture

The war in the Saturn system didn’t begin with a simple declaration. It began with a nutrient crisis on Earth, roughly two centuries in the making, a colonial logic that Earth never questioned until it was way too late, and a single chemical element that neither side can afford to truly lose control of. You can’t really understand why any of this is happening without understanding where it started - not on Titan, but on Earth, and in the political mathematics of a civilization that decided to scatter itself across the solar system before it had worked out what it owed the people it was scattering.

Why Earth Colonized the Outer System

Colonization of the solar system wasn’t a triumph of exploration. It was simply a managed emergency. By the late 21st century, population pressure, ecosystem degradation, and resource depletion had made the political case for off-world expansion irresistible. Governments that could barely cooperate on anything else managed to align behind a single project: scatter enough of humanity across enough rocks that no single catastrophe could end the species. The colonists who left were framed as - and largely understood themselves - as pioneers.

Economically, they were a relief valve. The colonies absorbed population pressure, opened new resource frontiers for corporations, and provided Earth’s governments with a political narrative of progress at a moment when it was stalling domestically. The colonists weren’t wrong to feel like pioneers. But they were also, structurally, an extraction mechanism. The resources they developed flowed straight back toward Earth. And the profits went to institutions whose headquarters weren’t even on Titan.

The British Parallel

The colonial logic follows a pattern that Earth had already run once: establish settlements to generate wealth for the home power, permit enough local governance to reduce administration costs, and assume that distance and dependency will prevent the colonies from ever becoming a genuine political threat. Britain made this calculation about the Americas. Earth reached the outer system. The outcome is rhyming so far.

The Phosphorus Problem

Phosphorus isn’t a dramatic element. What it does is underpin all biological life - it’s essential to DNA, ATP energy transfer, bone structure, and fertilizer for every crop that feeds every person on every world. There’s no substitute. You can’t engineer around it. Every closed ecological system, every aeroponic farm, every cultured meat vat, every human body in the Saturn system requires a continuous phosphorus supply to function.

For most of the colonization era, Earth had a near-monopoly on the supply of processed phosphorus to the outer system. This wasn’t accidental. It was the single most important economic lever Earth retained over colonies that were otherwise becoming self-sufficient in energy, food production, and manufacturing. You could build your own reactors. You could grow your own food. But you still needed Earth’s phosphorus to replenish the nutrients the closed loop inevitably lost over time, and Earth knew it.

The separatist movement in the outer systems had been building for generations - cultural distance, economic resentment, the memory of Rhea - but it had no viable path to actual independence as long as the dependency held. The moment that changed everything was the confirmation that carbonaceous asteroids, particularly C-type bodies in the Belt and Saturnian moons themselves, contained extractable phosphorus in quantities sufficient to break Earth’s monopoly. The discovery spread quickly. It was tested. It worked. And almost immediately, war broke out for sovereignty across the outer systems.

Why Earth Can't Simply Back Down

From Earth’s perspective, phosphorus isn’t just an economic asset. It’s the last lever. If the outer system develops independent phosphorus supply chains, there’s nothing left that compels their cooperation. The ECA’s hardliners understand that accepting Titan’s independence means accepting the eventual independence of every colony in the outer system - and the permanent loss of the resource base that Earth’s population still depends on stabilizing its own agricultural systems. The war isn’t about pride. It’s about whether Earth survives the next century as a functional location for civilization.

The Solar System in 2184

The Inner system didn’t exactly separate. When the phosphorus leverage broke, the result wasn’t a unified independence movement - it was a smooth transition. Mars and the inner system colonies were too economically integrated with Earth, too dependent on Earth manufacturing, and too vulnerable to rapid military response to allow for a successful secession. The inner system largely stayed under Earth’s political framework, with increasing local autonomy and growing tension.

The outer system - beyond the asteroid belt, where Earth’s military response times are measured in months to years, and the economic leverage is weaker - is fractured. Some colonies declared independence and held it. Others declared independence, lost the Civil War, and were reabsorbed under more punitive terms. Others Balkanized entirely: clashing ideologies, ethnic and religious divisions, disputes over resources so minor by Earth standards that they barely registered, but enormous in a sealed habitat where every centimeter of territory is life-support infrastructure. The outer system in 2184 isn’t a unified bloc opposing Earth. It’s a collection of successful independent states, failed states, contested territories, and communities still trying to work out what they want - with Earth watching all of it and deciding where to apply pressure.

The three largely successful independent states in the outer system are Titan, Europa, and Ganymede. The TFS is aware of this. Every functioning independent state makes Earth’s argument - that independence leads to chaos - harder to sustain. Titan winning isn’t just about Titan.

Earth in 2184

The Earth’s public lives in 2184 is not the same as it was when those first colonial missions launched. The climate crisis of the late 21st century was not averted; instead, it was simply survived and endured. Multiple breadbasket regions collapsed over roughly two decades, producing food and water scarcity at a scale that permanently reshaped where the world's populations could even live. Mass migration movements, organized and unorganized alike, changed the human geography of the planet. The population projected to reach ten billion peaked well short of that and declined before restabilizing. That gap between projections and reality is represented by the people who died, primarily during the transitional decades.

The acute phase is over. What remains is the management of the aftermath: extreme weather now being anticipated and partially mitigated via climatary engineering rather than purely unexpected; collapsed ecosystems where only the urban-adapted or migrated species survived; and a certain level of chronic food and water scarcity rather than the catastrophic state it was in previously. New breadbasket regions were established through GMO interventions to produce food, but without the margins that pre-crisis agriculture once had. Earth in 2184 is a civilization that survived something that should have been way worse, and it is still processing what that cost.

Within this context, the outer system colonies are just not optional. The phosphorus from Saturnian C-type bodies isn’t an economic asset in the sense that losing it would only reduce profits. It’s a material input to the food systems, keeping Earth’s population fed. Titan’s hydrocarbons feed manufacturing infrastructure that Earth’s own resource base can no longer fully support. When the ECA describes the outer system as infrastructure essential to Earth’s recovery, there is a reason: it’s because it's true. The generation that remembers the breadbasket collapse is much older now, but the institutional memory of the events remains.

Earth’s public doesn’t, for the most part, follow the affairs of the outer system closely. The colonies are distant, the issues are technical, and the ECA frames everything to minimize sympathy at all costs: rogue assets, stabilization operations, protection of infrastructure. Titan isn’t framed as a civilization asserting its right to exist; It’s framed as resource disruption, and on a planet still managing the aftermath of near-collapse, that framing nails the landing for the public. This has majority support on Earth, somewhat roughly equivalent to how a colonial power’s public typically relates to a distant suppressed uprising, until the images become undeniable. Which is why the ECA works hard to control said image.

The institution and the public are genuinely aligned on one point: the outer systems matter, its resources matter, and losing access to them is just not acceptable. The disagreements, both internal and public, are entirely about method and the cost of doing so.

The Rhea-Titan Economic Bond

Before the Butchering, Rhea and Titan had an emerging economic relationship that the history of the conflict tends to obscure. Rhea’s surface ice was a viable source of oxygen for Titan - low escape velocity on Rhea made ice export economically realistic, and Titan’s thick atmosphere made the transfer of resources between the two worlds about as viable as space colonization. This was more than a trade relationship. It was the foundation of a genuinely independent outer-system economy: Titan provided hydrocarbons and manufacturing, and Rhea provided oxygen as a feedstock.

Earth understood this. And so the Butchering wasn’t only a response to Rhea’s political declaration. It was the destruction of an economic relationship that, if it had matured, would have made the phosphorus question moot an entire generation earlier. The Rhea survivors who came to Titan knew exactly what had been destroyed and why. The TFS’s current push to establish Titan’s own oxygen supply - the nuclear ramjet research, the sub-surface drilling programs - is a direct continuation of what Rhea and Titan had been building before 2142.

Gravity, Bodies, and the Long-Term Human Cost

One thing Earth’s original planners underestimated was the physiological cost of low gravity across generations. Spaceflight osteoporosis - bone density loss from reduced gravitational loading - was documented from the earliest orbital missions. What wasn’t fully anticipated was the compounding effect across generations born in low-g environments who never experienced Earth's gravity at all.

Titan-born residents at 0.14g develop skeletal structures that are measurably different from those of Earth-born people. Their bones work fine for their environment, but are genuinely fragile in Earth gravity - the weight of a full-g environment on a Titan-born skeleton would be painful and potentially injurious without extended preparation. People can move from Earth to Titan, adapt over time, and eventually become Titan-bodied. People born on Titan can’t simply go to Earth. They’re, in a physiological sense, already a different population. Some Habs implement systems of pseudo-gravity - with rotating sections - to mitigate the effect in children. Others have accepted the adaptation as permanent. The practical consequence is that Titan-born people aren’t just politically separate from Earth. They’re physically committed to staying separate.

03

Titan — The Amber World

Primary Setting

Titan is Saturn’s largest moon and the beating heart of outer-system civilization. It has a thick nitrogen atmosphere denser than Earth’s, opaque orange skies that filter sunlight into a permanent amber haze, and lakes of liquid methane sprawling across its surface. It’s cold enough to kill exposed skin in seconds. And 6.2 million people call it home.

The sky here is a color that has no name in Standard. The Old Earth colonists kept calling it orange, amber, or even rusty. We just call it sky. We don't compare it to anything; we compare things to it.

— Hazeport resident, interviewed 2183

Physical Environment

Titan’s atmosphere is 95% nitrogen with methane clouds and complex organic chemistry in the upper layers. Atmospheric pressure at the surface is 1.45 bar - much denser than Earth’s, which actually reduces suit complexity compared to vacuum environments, but the chemical composition and the cold are still instantly lethal. Gravity is 0.14g, meaning you weigh about a seventh of what you would on Earth. Long-term residents are measurably different from Earth-born people: slightly longer limbs, lower bone density, and a cardiovascular system adapted to lower circulatory demands. None of this is pathological in Titan’s environment. All of it becomes a problem the moment someone tries to go back.

Gravity Note - Roleplay Implication

At 0.14g, movement feels floaty but not weightless. You can jump several meters easily. Heavy objects are manageable one-handed. Titan-born characters often find Earth footage unsettling - all that weight, all that compression. Some have never been off-moon and find even simulated Martian gravity is deeply uncomfortable in training environments.

Habitats

No one lives truly on the surface. Titan’s habitats are built subsurface, carved from ice and rock, or the pressurized tunnels laced between domed anchor-structures. The major population centers are connected by pressurized transit tunnels - narrow, functional, lit by bioluminescent strip panels. The architecture is utilitarian by necessity and by culture: thick walls, low ceilings, every centimeter accounted for. Newer domes in the wealthier districts have glass observation panels fitted with atmospheric scrubbers, where citizens can look out at methane lakes and amber clouds without dying. Older habitats don’t have this luxury.

Power comes from a strong percentage of geothermal taps, with hydrocarbon combustion as backup - Titan’s methane is effectively a limitless fuel source, though it’s also its primary export commodity, which is central to the conflict with Earth. Nuclear fission supplies larger Hab complexes and spacecraft; reactor coolant is easily sourced from the water ice throughout the moon’s crust, and Titan’s thick atmosphere makes coolant management more efficient than on Earth. The electrical distribution grid runs largely outside the Habs, exploiting Titan’s ambient cold as a natural superconductor coolant - a counterintuitive engineering choice that dramatically improves grid efficiency but can leave power infrastructure exposed to surface conditions.

Two options that might seem obvious aren’t viable. Splitting water into hydrogen and oxygen for fuel requires more energy than the combustion returns - even at perfect efficiency, you’d break even at best, meaning the process consumes the colony’s power rather than producing it. And burning the hydrocarbons themselves requires oxygen, of which Titan’s atmosphere has none in useful quantities. The abundant fuel is effectively inert without an oxidizer. This is why the Rhea oxygen trade mattered so much: Rhea’s cleaner ice was a supply of oxidizer that could have unlocked Titan’s hydrocarbon energy fully. Its destruction removed that option. The TFS’s research programs include multiple long-term efforts to develop an indigenous oxygen supply, including a theoretical nuclear ramjet design that could harvest atmospheric compounds from high altitude, but none are at operational scale in 2184.

Hazeport - The Capital

The largest single Hab-complex on Titan, home to roughly 800,000 people. Built around the original colonial landing infrastructure, it has grown in concentric rings of varying architectural quality - gleaming pressurized glass and recycled aluminum in the inner rings, dense improvised sprawl in the outer ones. The Assembly broadcasts from Hazeport. Earth’s intelligence cells are most concentrated here. The methane markets are here. So is most of the poverty.

Kraken Mare

The largest of Titan’s methane seas. The Kraken Mare accord - signed in 2180 - unified 82 of Titan’s 95 major habitats under the Titan Assembly. The sea is now patrolled by Assembly-aligned craft. The ECA keeps drones above it. Somewhere in the depths, the Assembly is said to be constructing plasma rail defense platforms.

The Other Moons

Saturn System

Titan is the center of the revolution, but the Saturnian system is a constellation of distinct worlds. Each one is shaped by its geology, economy, and relationship to the forces tearing the system apart. A character from Enceladus is not a character from Tethys. The moon you come from is the first thing about you that anyone reads.

Mimas
Corporate
Pop. ~50,000 // Corporate-Operated // Alignment: Unstable
Surface area slightly less than Spain // Composition: water ice with trace surface rock // Gravity: 0.064g // No atmosphere
The edge of the edge. Mimas is what happens when a corporation decides a moon is a resource and its workers are a logistical problem to be solved at minimum cost. The surface is pocked with craters, the architecture is buried or domed, and the glow-panel lighting in every corridor does a poor job of compensating because you are living inside the ice at the bottom of someone else’s financial model. There is no culture here that wasn’t smuggled in. There is no art that wasn’t made in secret. The people who end up on Mimas are people who ran out of options somewhere else - refugees, contract workers whose contracts weren’t what they were told, people fleeing something on a moon that still had a choice. The morale is low in how permanent cold is: it doesn’t spike, it just never lifts.
Politically, Mimas is volatile precisely because it has so little to lose. ECA enforcement is present but thin. The Kosmo Mauraders use their transit infrastructure more freely than anyone officially acknowledges. TFS sympathizers operate quietly in the labor housing. The Herschel Crater rim is the closest thing to a political center - whoever controls the mining operations there controls what little economic life exists.
Named Locations
Herschel Station / Rimtown
Mining HQ // Crater Rim Settlement
The largest population center is built into the walls of Herschel Crater. The crater’s natural walls provide protection from radiation and micro-impacts; the deeper layers expose rock for mining. A huge scarred basin of lights and machinery. Risk of ice-quake activity. The de facto capital of Mimas, which means very little.
Krivos Basin Complex
Mid-size Equatorial Settlement
Built beneath a cluster of smaller craters in the more thermally stable equatorial zone. Excavated with ice-boring robots. The most livable area on Mimas by a low standard. Longer-term residents, some families. Also, the location referenced in the 2174 Ice Cutter Revolt - Krivos miners were among the first to act.
Northpoint / Polaris Extract
Polar Ice Mining // Strip Settlement
The north polar plains have thick, clean ice ideal for off-world export. Linear strip-cities running parallel to ice fracture lines for efficient harvesting. Almost entirely automated rigs and temporary worker housing. The people here rotate every six months. Nobody stays by choice.
Edgeport Mimas
Transit Relay // Staging Hub
A small transit hub exploiting Mimas’s stable orbit for refueling and inter-moon staging. Populated by logistics crews and engineers. Also used by the Kosmo Mauraders as a waypoint that ECA patrols cover infrequently. Known to anyone who moves contraband through the system.
Corporate Extraction
Enceladus
Earth-Controlled
Pop. ~310,000 // Corporate-ECA Aligned // Alignment: Earth
Geologically active // Tiger Stripe plumes at south pole // Confirmed subsurface liquid ocean // Gravity: 0.113g
The most advanced and centralized moon in the system outside Titan and Iapetus. Enceladus is a corporate stronghold built around one extraordinary geological fact: it is active. The Tiger Stripe fractures at the south pole erupt continuously, feeding the E-Ring of Saturn and providing effectively limitless clean water - tapped via capture towers and pressurized vents, purified on-site, and shipped across the system. The surface infrastructure is industrial and modular, built to withstand plume shockwaves and geyser shifts. Beneath the surface, the architecture is something else entirely: elegant, curved, leveraging the ice’s plasticity and the warmth rising from the moon’s heart. There is beauty here. It’s owned by the Consortium.
In 2180, Enceladus aligned formally with the ECA, choosing corporate incentives and proximity to Earth power over solidarity with Titan. The population is not unanimous; a significant minority views this as capitulation, and the pro-Assembly underground operates carefully in the lower cavern settlements. The Black Tides Fleet is now staged here. Enceladus has become, whether its population chose it or not, the launching platform for the invasion of Titan.
Named Locations
Cryoport Vega / Plumehold
South Pole // Primary Settlement // Water Export Hub
The major population center was built around the Tiger Stripe capture towers. Water harvesting, purification, and export logistics dominate. Direct subsurface ocean access is restricted to corporate science teams. The most ECA-controlled location on the moon - heavily monitored since the blockade began.
Aquatide / Thermora / Nerra Deep
Sub-Ice Cavern Settlements // Tidal Thermal Habitats
Warmed by tidal heating and protected from radiation, these mid-sized cavern cities are Enceladus at its most remarkable - long-term habitation, biotech R&D, and the only place in the outer system where someone can stand in a space that feels genuinely natural. The TFS underground on Enceladus is most active in Nerra Deep.
Midwhite Station / Helion Ridge
Equatorial Surface Domes // Worker and Logistics Housing
Stable terrain, minimal geyser risk, consistent lighting from Saturn’s reflected glow. Domed towns supporting workers and corporate logistics. More comfortable than Mimas; less interesting than the caverns. The people here mostly prefer not to have opinions.
E-Ring Station Perseus
Orbital Platform // Shipyard and Command Node
The orbital hub monitors and regulates ice shipments outbound from Enceladus. Stations, data infrastructure, fuel depots. Currently also serving as the logistics node for the Black Tides Fleet staging. The most heavily militarized piece of infrastructure in the Saturn system outside Tethys.
Sagan Deepwatch / Fracture Site Theta
Research Sanctuaries // Restricted Access
High-security science installations run by intersystem universities and private biotech firms, positioned at active plume vents with direct ocean access. Secretive, politically sensitive. Whether microbial life has been confirmed here is one of the questions the Consortium has declined to answer publicly for nearly eleven years.
ECA Staging Ground
Tethys
Occupied
Pop. ~800,000 // ECA Penal Colony // Alignment: Enforced
Nearly pure water ice // Odysseus Crater covers 18% of surface // Ithaca Chasma rift spans ~2,000 km // Gravity: 0.145g
The Tortured Moon. Earth has held Tethys from its first day of colonization, and the infrastructure installed here was never designed for the welfare of its residents. The dominant feature of the surface is Odysseus Crater, a basin covering nearly a fifth of the entire moon; its interior walls now host the primary penal complex. Ithaca Chasma, a canyon rift wrapping most of the moon, serves as a transit spine. The architecture is subterranean bunkers and frozen-over metal, heat-haze vents over frostbitten landscapes, surveillance drones and searchlights, and harsh klaxons that echo through the ice into your suit. Cold, militarized, morally hollow.
Most of the 800,000 residents of Tethys did not choose to be here. The population is prisoners, guards, administrative staff, corporate ice-mining contractors on reduced-sentence work programs, and a small but growing underground of people who have been here long enough to learn what this place is and decide to do something about it. Characters who have served time on Tethys and returned are among the most practically useful people in the revolution - they understand ECA systems, detention procedures, and pressure from the inside.
Named Locations
Scheria Prime / Echo-1 Detainment Arc
Primary Penal Complex // Odysseus Crater Interior
The crater’s natural walls - the Scheria Montes - serve as fortress walls. The interior is divided into security tiers. Automated sentry towers, vertical burrows carved into the ice, drop cells melted into the crater floor. Brutalist structures half-buried in ice, powered by external reactor towers. Zero privacy. Constant surveillance. The largest single concentration of political prisoners in the outer system.
Ithaca Conduit / Line-9 Spine
Transit Corridor // 2,000 km Maglev System
A sealed maglev track running through the entire length of Ithaca Chasma, connecting population sectors for prisoner transfer and resource logistics in pressurized train pods. Auxiliary prison units and isolated deep cells are embedded at intervals. An iconic visual: a long transit line in an icy trench stretching to the horizon in both directions. The Conduit is also the primary artery of the Tethys underground; information and contraband travel through it in both directions.
Leaseblock Theta / Z12 Extractor Field
Corporate Ice Mining // Equatorial Lease Zones
Short-term corporate leases for equatorial ice extraction. Modular stations with rotating staff - mostly indentured workers, contractors, and ex-inmates working for sentence reductions. Harsh, regulated, fenced. The people here know they are being used. Some of them are deciding what to do about it.
TOCP-1 / Cerberus Gate
Orbital Command Platform // Polar Synchronous Orbit
Earth’s primary orbital installation for Tethys operations: observation, communications, rapid-response strike, prisoner transfer coordination. Half administrative center, half a visible demonstration of force. Passes over Odysseus and the Ithaca Chasma on every orbit. The hardest single target to approach covertly in the Saturn system.
Frostshade / Outpost 47 / The Quiet Reach
Far Hemisphere // Abandoned / Unknown
Areas farthest from Earth’s operations, reportedly containing abandoned facilities, escaped prisoner hideouts, and resistance networks. Nothing is confirmed. The ECA’s surveillance coverage thins significantly in the far hemisphere. People disappear into it. Some of them seem to disappear on purpose.
Gulag Moon
Iapetus
Contested
Pop. ~2 million // Earth Capital / Cultural Twin to Titan // Alignment: Fractured
Dual-colored surface: dark organic/silicate leading hemisphere, bright ice trailing hemisphere // Voyager Mountains: 20 km tall, 1,300 km long equatorial ridge // Gravity: 0.223g
A moon of contrast - dark and light, wealth and labor, rule and resistance. A defining equatorial ridgeline, twenty kilometers high and thirteen hundred kilometers long, the Voyager Mountains rise from the dark organic terrain like a blade. Here is where the ECA has established its Saturnian capital: political delegates, corporate headquarters, embassies, and orbital elevators are housed in the ridge cities. The higher you live on the ridge, the more powerful you are. The lower tiers are industrial labor housing. From the constant mining activity, dust rains back down onto the city. The entire structure - social and geological - feels like it’s always slightly on the edge of something.
Culturally, Iapetus sees itself as Titan’s sister world, and the population’s sympathies are divided along lines that don’t map neatly to geography. The Aurora Compact operates heavily here precisely because of the access it provides to both ECA infrastructure and TFS sympathizers. Protests erupted in 2179 after Titan’s declaration; a significant pro-Assembly faction exists within even the ridge cities. The orbital elevator infrastructure makes Iapetus the most strategically important moon in the system after Titan - whoever controls the elevator controls the movement of goods through the entire region.
Named Locations
Ridgepoint / Darkspire / New Delphi
Voyager Ridge Megacity // ECA Capital // Millions of residents
The defining settlement of the Saturn system outside Titan. Arcologies and refineries built into the ridgeline, stratified by altitude - governance at the top, industrial labor at the bottom. Political delegates, corporate headquarters, embassies. Buzzing, sprawling, segmented. Class conflict is baked into the architecture. The Aurora Compact’s most public offices are here. So is the ECA’s Saturnian administrative headquarters.
Cassini Forge / Umbral Flats / Sector C-R01
Dark Hemisphere Lowlands // Industrial Strip Mining
The leading hemisphere’s dark organic and silicate material is the primary extraction target, refined into high-value compounds and shipped across the system. Open-pit mines, mobile refineries, and excavation machinery at city-scale. Transient labor population. The Umbral Flats are where most of the actual work gets done and where class consciousness runs hottest. Sabotage of refinery infrastructure in this zone has been increasing since 2180.
Penumbra Station / Janus Line / Midway Delta
Terminus Arc Rail // Day-Night Border Transit
The sharp albedo boundary between the two hemispheres hosts a massive transit corridor linking mining zones to orbital launch infrastructure. Mid-sized transit and engineering hubs along the equator: repair yards, cargo depots, traveler enclaves. The Janus Line is one of the few places on Iapetus where workers from both hemispheres regularly share space - and where information passes between factions with some freedom.
Selene Heights / Whiteport / The Lumen Arc
Bright Hemisphere // Elite Enclaves and Research Stations
The trailing ice hemisphere: high-albedo, quiet, isolated from the industrial darkness of the other side. Wealthy enclaves with views of Saturn and deep space. Military monitoring stations. Scientific observatories. The contrast with the Umbral Flats is the most visible expression of Iapetus’s inequality - you can see both hemispheres from the ridgeline, and the difference in light isn’t metaphorical.
Elevator 6 / Crownpost Alpha
Orbital Tether System // Mass Driver Launch Infrastructure
The only fully developed tether system in the Saturnian system. Ground-based accelerators launch cargo to orbital transfer stations; a tether network handles passenger and high-priority freight. Small, high-tech population of operators and maintenance crews. The single most strategically important piece of infrastructure on Iapetus. Controlling access to the elevator is controlling the economic artery of the entire system.
ECA Capital / TFS Sympathizers
Rhea
The Martyr Moon
Pop. ~0 permanent // Pilgrims, Archivists, Ghosts // ECA calls it: "Final Revolt Site"
Water ice surface with fracture cliff systems // Former population ~1.5 million // Nuked 2142 // Surface and subsurface contamination ongoing // Ice unusable for life support // Orbital debris field from strike still present
Rhea is not just a moon anymore. It is a wound. What happened here in 2142 is the defining political fact of the outer system - the event that made the current revolution not just possible, but inevitable. Earth calls it the Last Revolt. The rest of the Outer system calls it "The Butchering." The stark difference in language is derived from who has the right to name what was done.
The revolution on Rhea didn’t begin as a radical act. It began because the government Earth had permitted - a technically self-ruling but in practice a puppet administration - was corrupt, and the corruption got worse with every increase in nominal independence. The Revolutionaries attacked government buildings. Colonies embraced the new citizen governance. And Earth, watching a precedent form, responded with orbital strikes, mass arrests, torture, and finally, nuclear weapons against the major settlements that had fully revolted. Survivors were relocated to Tethys or rescued by sympathetic outer-system vessels. The moon’s orbital plane now carries debris from the strikes, a navigational hazard that has required every satellite in the system to be built to a new hardness standard.
On Earth, the action had strong public support as it was framed as counterterrorism. The nuclear use was eventually seen as excessive by a minority, but it was broadly accepted as necessary. The ECA now maintains a formal quarantine of the entire moon, citing its ongoing contamination, documented by independent analysis of smuggled samples. There is no unauthorized access. Full anti-contamination protocols for any authorized personnel. No surface materials to be removed. The contamination gives the quarantine a scientific justification that’s harder to argue with than its politics alone, and within that justification lies the thing the quarantine is also ensuring: no shrines, no public broadcasts using the name, and no pilgrimage. The ECA doesn’t have to say it’s suppressing commemoration; it just has to keep enforcing the quarantine.
Forty years out, the contamination profile isn’t what most people expect from a nuclear site. External radiation levels have dropped considerably. The danger isn’t proximity — it’s ingestion. Disturbed particulate, surface water, anything that gets inside a suit. The long-duration radioisotope contamination in the ice accumulates quietly, and the effects take years to surface. Independent researchers who’ve analyzed smuggled samples know roughly what’s in there; the specific composition is contested because the ECA disputes all independent methodology and has published nothing of its own. The Titan Free State — In Depth

The TFS isn't a government body that controls Titan, but rather the coalition of habitats across it that agreed to act together as one in a revolution against the ECA. Eighty-two of the ninety-five major habs of Titan signed the Kraken Doctrine in 2180 and collectively became the Titan Free State. Each of those habs retains its own local governance, rules, internal politics, and its own way of choosing who speaks for it. What they share is the Doctrine, the Assembly it created, and the commitment to treat an attack on any signatory as an attack on all of them.

This matters for understanding what the TFS can and can't do. It can mobilize collective military response, speak with a unified voice in negotiations, coordinate resource sharing, and organize resistance across the whole moon. What it can't do is tell a hab how to run itself. The TFS has no authority over local laws, customs, or governance decisions. A hab that elects via a council of engineers and a hab that elects by popular assembly are both full members with equal standing. This is deliberate — the thing they're fighting against is the ECA telling Titan and its people what to do, and the TFS has been careful not to recreate that relationship at a smaller scale.

The Kraken Doctrine

Before the TFS, there was the Doctrine — issued by the First Assembly in 2171, an act of high treason by any ECA legal standard. A formal declaration that the Earth Consolidated Authority had forfeited its governing authority over Titan and its habitats, citing a long record of economic extraction, political suppression, and deliberate harm. It resolved that the habitats of Titan were sovereign entities with the right to govern themselves, form their own compacts, and defend themselves by whatever means necessary. It named names, listed dates, and described the kill protocols in the oxygen firmware two years before Day Zero proved the point.

The Doctrine wasn't a constitution. It didn't establish institutions or define rights in legal detail — that work came later and is still ongoing. What it did was commit. It drew a line and said we are on this side of it now, and there is no returning. The ECA banned it within days. Within weeks, "We are done waiting" was graffiti on every hab wall in the system. The opening line has been quoted at Assembly sessions and painted on ship and hab hulls:

We do not seek conflict. We only seek what was promised to every person who left Earth with nothing but the expectation that their labor would earn them a life worth living. That promise was broken, and we are done waiting for it to be kept.

— The Kraken Doctrine, preamble, 2171

The Doctrine is also contested on the grounds of what it actually means. Some habs read it as a declaration of full independence — permanent, non-negotiable, no reconciliation possible. Others read the grievance list as the point, not the separation itself: if the ECA addressed the grievances, separation might not be necessary. This split runs through every major Assembly debate about negotiation, ceasefire, and what a settlement would actually have to contain to be acceptable.

The Assembly

The Assembly is the legislature of the TFS — the body where the eighty-two signatory habs meet, debate, and vote. It broadcasts from Hazeport, though the Doctrine allows it to convene anywhere on Titan in an emergency. Each hab sends at least one delegate. How that delegate is chosen is entirely the hab's business — some elect them, some appoint them, some rotate the role through their hab council. The Assembly doesn't standardize this, which means the legitimacy of individual delegates varies considerably and is occasionally contested.

Voting operates on a qualified majority system with two simultaneous thresholds. The hab threshold treats each signatory hab as a single vote regardless of size — a hab of three thousand people and Hazeport both count as one hab-vote. The population threshold weights votes by the number of adult TFS citizens each hab represents. Major decisions — military action, treaty negotiations, resource commitments, anything that binds the coalition — require a qualified majority on both measures simultaneously. Either threshold alone can block a decision. Minor administrative matters clear on simpler terms.

Each hab casts its single hab-vote as a unit, decided internally by whatever process the hab uses. For population weighting, each hab's delegation carries the population count of its adult TFS citizens. Holdout hab residents are noncitizens of the TFS and don't factor into population totals unless a signatory hab has individually naturalized them — which individual habs can choose to do, by their own criteria, without Assembly approval.

For decisions with more than two options, the Assembly uses ranked-choice voting, resolved quickly by the same technology infrastructure that handles life-support monitoring across the colony. Votes don't take days. Political maneuvering that determines outcomes happens before a vote is called, not during counting.

The Speaker

The Speaker of the Assembly chairs the sessions, controls the agenda, coordinates between votes, and speaks publicly for Assembly positions. Elected by the full delegate body and serving a two-year term. The role has no binding authority — the Speaker can't make decisions the Assembly hasn't voted on — but agenda control is the actual power. What gets scheduled, when, in what order, framed how. Speakers who understand this serve effectively. Speakers who think the role is ceremonial don't last.

The Military Council

The Assembly can't move fast enough for military decisions. The Military Council exists to fill that gap — a body authorized to approve emergency military responses without waiting for a full Assembly session. Its composition isn't fixed. The Council forms around each situation from the habs most capable of responding and most directly affected: whoever can get ships there fastest, whoever controls the infrastructure at stake, whoever has people on the ground in the affected area. A hab on the far side of Titan from an engagement doesn't have a seat at that particular council, but a hab that's directly threatened does — even if they only have two ships.

Any Military Council decision is subject to full Assembly ratification within a defined period afterward. Most decisions get ratified because they're taken in response to situations the Assembly would have approved anyway. The political tension is about cases that aren't clear-cut — emergency authorizations that turn out to be more consequential than they appeared, that benefit some habs at the expense of others, or surprise engagements that some would have liked to discuss more. The concern isn't that any single hab is accumulating formal power — the Council has no permanent membership. The concern is that the habs with the most ships keep ending up in the same room, making decisions that happen to serve their interests, and calling it an emergency.

The TFS Navy

The TFS didn't build a navy — it simply recognized one. Before the Assembly formalized anything, there were already armed vessels operating in Titan's orbital space and methane seas: converted haulers, independently owned gunships, and former Kosmo Marauder craft whose captains aligned with the cause more than the payment. The Assembly's naval authority is a formal structure sitting on top of something that was already operating without it, and the fit is imperfect.

There is no admiral or chain of command above fleet level. What exists is a loose agreement among the captains and commanders of significant TFS vessels about what they're trying to accomplish and a rough idea of how. The habs with the most ships have the most pull over collective decisions — not because anyone gave them that authority, but because when you're engaging an ECA patrol, the person with six ships matters more than the person with one. The plasma rail platforms beneath Kraken Mare answer to their local hab council, not to any fleet authority. The TFS Navy can request their support but can't order it.

Coordination happens through the Military Council when there's an Assembly-level decision to make, and through direct communication between captains when there isn't. The Battle of the Kraken Strait in 2183 was planned and executed by two hab fleets without formal authorization — they informed the Assembly afterward. The Assembly ratified it. This is increasingly the pattern.

The Holdout Habs

Thirteen habs didn't sign the Kraken Doctrine. Two were actively ECA-aligned — passing information, hosting enforcement units, functioning as operational nodes for the blockade. The Assembly authorized military action against both. They are now administered directly by Assembly-appointed councils, previous governance suspended, TFS militia providing security. They have no Assembly representation of their own. Other habs can and do occasionally raise their situation in debate — the Doctrine's commitment to Titanian self-determination creates an obvious tension with indefinite military administration of two hab populations who didn't choose it. What happens to those habs when the war ends is an unresolved question the Assembly has declined to address formally.

The remaining eleven habs wanted to stay out — not ECA-aligned, just unwilling to commit to a war they weren't sure they could survive. The TFS recognizes they don't wish to be part of the wider coalition and the Assembly has stopped short of forcing accession. It hasn't left them alone. Military presence exists at every connection point to transit infrastructure — at perimeters, at docking approaches, at the corridors linking these habs to the rest of the moon. Framed as blockade enforcement, ensuring ECA assets don't use neutral habs as cover. The TFS controls what comes in and out, and doesn't pretend otherwise. The arrangement persists because the alternative — absorbing them by force or leaving the perimeter gaps genuinely open — is worse for everyone.

These habs are called Observer Habs in Assembly discussion, a term that papers over how little they actually observe. They run their own local governance without TFS interference, receive no Assembly services, and carry none of its obligations. Their residents are noncitizens unless individually naturalized by a signatory hab — which a slow but steady number have chosen to pursue, gradually changing the political character of those communities without anyone formally changing their status.

Internal Fractures

Eighty-two habs agreeing on independence doesn't mean they agree on much else. The TFS is ideologically fragmented at the local level — each hab brings its own history, economic pressures, and understanding of what they're actually fighting for. This isn't organized into named factions with platforms. It's the accumulated friction of communities sharing a cause but not a shared culture.

Some habs take a hard line on ECA contact: no trade, no negotiations, no communication beyond demands. Others maintain quiet back-channels because they need supplies, or because they're not convinced this ends well and want options. Some habs have strong Rhea memorial culture and treat any suggestion of a negotiated settlement as a betrayal of the dead. Others are more pragmatic — what matters is independence, not punishment, and if the ECA offers real terms, they'd consider them. Some habs have tight economic controls and collective ownership of life-support infrastructure. Others run more like markets with private guild contracts. When Assembly delegates from these habs argue over resource-sharing policy, they're not just arguing policy — they're arguing about what kind of society Titan should be.

The military question produces the sharpest friction. Habs with ships and combat-experienced crews have disproportionate influence on what the TFS actually does, regardless of how Assembly votes fall. Habs without significant military assets feel this. The smaller habs didn't sign the Doctrine to become clients of the larger ones. The Assembly debates about Military Council authority and ratification widows are, underneath the procedural language, arguments about exactly this.

The Assembly's History

The Assembly didn't begin as an institution. It began as a meeting. Each structural decision was a response to something that had just gone wrong, or something about to.

An informal underground forum starts convening in the deeper dome sections of Hazeport: philosophers, engineers, Rhea survivors, guild representatives, people who had spent years talking on the ghost webs and finally decided to be in the same room. No delegates, no voting procedures, no formal membership, no name. Attendance is irregular and is itself a security risk. What it has is ghost web infrastructure to communicate between sessions and a shared understanding that something needs to exist that doesn't yet.

The forum produces one significant output: the Kraken Doctrine. Drafted collectively across several sessions, issued under the name "The First Assembly of Titan" — the first time the group names itself, and also the last thing the First Assembly does as a coherent body. Earth bans it within days. The forum goes back underground, now carrying a name, a founding document, and every ECA intelligence unit on Titan looking for its members. Several are arrested in the months that follow. The rest learn to be more careful.

What it does is function as a coordination node: routing information between habs through the ghost webs, supporting the investigation that uncovers the kill protocol buried in the oxygen firmware, circulating the findings before Earth can contain them.

The Ice Cutter Revolt isn't Assembly-organized, but the Assembly uses the newly emerging Titan Free Network to amplify it: broadcasting the martyr's declaration, coordinating responses across habs, carrying the story to settlements ECA censorship would otherwise have silenced. These aren't delegates in any formal sense — there's no process, no credentials — but the pattern of representation has begun.

The decision to publish the 2177 manifesto is the first the Assembly makes collectively with something resembling a vote: attendance at the session, a show of hands, marking the transition from forum to body. The manifesto is signed by representatives from 31 habs. Not all are authorized by their councils to sign. Some do it on their own initiative and deal with the political consequences afterward. A few habs quietly disavow their representative's signature. Most don't. Going public means Earth now treats Assembly membership as sedition.
2178

The ECA's rogue asset declaration and the sanctions that follow force the Assembly into real governance decisions: coordinating supply sharing between habs, organizing collective responses to blockade pressure, deciding which ECA infrastructure can be safely sabotaged and which can't. These decisions need a process. The show-of-hands system no longer holds. Over the course of 2178, representatives from the larger habs draft what will become the ratified Kraken Doctrine — a formal structure for the coalition. The draft circulates through hab councils across Titan for months before the independence declaration.
2179
Independence and the First Formal Election
The independence declaration is issued on the 37th anniversary of Rhea. Behind the public act, the Assembly runs its first structured process: hab councils formally nominate delegates, an adult population count is taken across participating habs, and the declaration goes to a vote under provisional rules drawn from the Doctrine draft. It passes. The count is contested in at least three cases. Two delegates vote without confirmed hab council authorization. Some habs are better represented than others. None of this stops the declaration. 2180
The Kraken Doctrine — Constitutional Moment
Eighty-two habs sign the Doctrine over six weeks — some immediately, some only after protracted internal debate. The Doctrine codifies the dual-threshold voting system, establishes the Speaker's two-year term, and defines the limits of Assembly authority over member habs. It explicitly excludes local governance, local law, and internal hab decisions from Assembly reach — a clause several larger habs pushed for to prevent Hazeport from using coalition membership as leverage over smaller settlements. 2181
The Military Council Forms Under Pressure
The full blockade begins in 2181. Within weeks it's clear the Assembly's voting process is too slow for military decisions — a full session requires notice, quorum, and debate the military situation doesn't always allow. Several smaller habs vote against it, arguing correctly that the habs with the most ships will dominate it regardless of formal rules. Current State
The Assembly governs openly from Hazeport. The second Speaker election under the Doctrine is due within the year — the current Speaker's term ends in late 2184, and the campaign to replace her has already started in hab council politics across the coalition. The Military Council's composition has shifted three times since 2181 as TFS naval capacity has changed. The prior authorization motion is pending. The Black Tides Fleet is at Enceladus. The Assembly is about to find out whether the structures it built can hold the weight of an actual invasion.
For Characters and GMs

The TFS works best as a setting element when it's neither a heroic resistance nor a functional government — a coalition of people who are genuinely right about the ECA and genuinely struggling with what it means to build something better under fire. A character aligned with the TFS has to decide which part of it they're aligned with: the Doctrine's ideals, their specific hab's interests, the military network, the Assembly's procedural legitimacy, or just the shared enemy. These aren't always the same thing. The richest TFS characters are the ones who notice the gap.

The Earth Consolidated Authority — In Depth

The ECA is not a government like most historical governments were. It didn’t emerge from a revolution, a constitution, or a popular mandate. It emerged from an emergency: a series of overlapping crises in the late 21st and early 22nd centuries that required a coordinated response beyond the capacity of existing national governments. Each time an emergency passed, the authority created to manage it remained in place. What has accumulated over a century functions like a government, is staffed by people who believe they are governing legitimately, and has never put its own legitimacy to a public vote.

However, cynicism is not the philosophical underpinning of the public. The ECA’s institutional culture genuinely believes that complex systems (planetary climate management, interstellar resource logistics, population distribution across the solar system) require expert administration, not purely democratic deliberation. The public is understood almost as a stakeholder: to be informed selectively, consulted occasionally, and managed continuously. This isn’t framed as contempt; it’s instead framed as a responsibility. The people running the ECA believe they are doing difficult, unglamorous work that keeps eight billion people alive, and they are not entirely wrong.

The Director

The ECA is formally headed by a Director, appointed to a five-year term with the possibility of a second term. Following the UN Secretary-General model, institutional blocs with considerable influence propose candidates; the most influential blocs possess informal veto power; and private discussions continue until candidates are found that no major bloc will actively reject.

The Director does have active authority; binding sanctions, authorizing military actions, and creating peacekeeping mandates. But it is bound by the coalition that produced it. A director who loses the confidence of the major institutional blocks before their term ends doesn’t get formally removed. But they will find that their authorizations are slow to be executed, their recommendations quietly deferred, and the machinery of the ECA begins to route around them. The position requires constant political maintenance, and the Directors who understand this govern effectively.

The Internal Split

The ECA is not unified on Titan. The internal disagreement runs deep and predates the current military standoff.

The hardline position holds that, if permitted, Titan’s independence will become a template for others. Every outer system colony that wants to break away will point to Titan as proof that the ECA will eventually accept some form of separation. The resources lost won’t be just the ones on Titan. They’ll mark the beginning of a progressive loss of access to the outer system that Earth simply cannot absorb. This position argues for a fast, decisive military resolution before a precedent is set.

The moderate position doesn’t dispute the stakes. It disputes the method. A military resolution that produces civilian casualties at scale will become visible to the Earth’s public in a way the ECA can’t manage, and that creates a political cost that outlasts the military victory. The major constraint on replicating what happened with Rhea is that while it was considered a military success, it became a political catastrophe. Moderates want a negotiated outcome that preserves resource access without the long-term radicalization that tends to be the result of another visible atrocity.

A smaller position within the bureaucracy holds that Titan’s grievances are substantially legitimate: that the economic extraction model, the infrastructure dependency, and certain decisions made about life-support systems in the outer colonies have crossed lines that the ECA’s own internal standards prohibit. While this position doesn’t necessarily argue for independence, it argues that the ECA needs to address what it has actually done before it can credibly argue for a continued authority over governance. It is not a popular position to openly hold, and those who do tend to be careful about where they say so.

The information gap around The Day Zero Event shapes everything, yet no one on Earth fully understands it. The ECA’s official position, both publicly and internally, is that the simultaneous life-support failures across seventeen habitats were caused by software conflicts in Earth-supplied systems. Most people arguing about policy regarding Titan on Earth believe this. What they know is that something went badly wrong, that thirty thousand people died, and that the TFS cites the event constantly as evidence of deliberate harm. The ECA officials who know the actual content of the investigation findings, that a kill protocol was installed remotely and then deliberately activated, are not allowed to be in the rooms where most of these policy debates take place.

Day Zero — The Information Gap

In the outer system, what happened on Day Zero is well understood. The investigators published their findings through the ghost webs before Earth could contain them. Thirty thousand dead, and a kill protocol built into the oxygen firmware and deliberately activated. Every adult on Titan lived through it or knows someone who did. And in the inner system and on Earth, the official account of software malfunction has never been publicly challenged by anyone with the authority to make the challenge stick. The department responsible knows; a few senior ECA figures know; the director does not know; and the admiral commanding the Black Tides Fleet does not know. They are making decisions about a war whose defining atrocity they misunderstand on a fundamental level.

The Black Tides Fleet - Command and Constraint

The political commissar attached to the Black Tides Fleet is not a part of a standing ECA institution. Their Political officers are embedded in a military command specifically for crisis situations, particularly when an engagement is sensitive enough that a civilian authority needs to act as a direct representative in the operational structure rather than just receiving reports after the fact. The commissar was appointed when the fleet was first authorized, and specifically because the Rhea Constraint makes orbital bombardment a decision with severe political consequences that a military commander alone shouldn’t be making.

The commissar has the authority to countermand orders that could lead to another Rhea event. The commissar’s reliance on the admiral’s staff for information is its structural limitation. What the commissar knows about the military situation, about the actions being considered, and about their consequences comes through channels the admirals control. And a commissar who understands this carefully builds a suite of independent information sources. A commissar who doesn’t is functionally decorative.

The admirals tend to believe that a fast and overwhelming strike that targets military infrastructure while minimizing civilian exposure would end the conflict before it becomes a protracted occupation that no one wins. The commissar believes that “minimizing civilian exposure” in a colony where military and civilian infrastructure share the same systems is not a promise anyone can keep, and that the political cost of being wrong falls on Earth, and not on the fleet.

Enforcement and Colonial Law

The legal framework governing ECA operations in the outer system is the Earth Colonial Code, a body of law accumulated over a century of colonial administration. Its structure reflects the ECA’s core assumption that colonial populations are subjects to be administered rather than citizens to be governed. Enforcement personnel have enormous discretionary authority. Detention without charge is a standard procedure. The practical floor of enforcement is whatever the contractor on the ground decides to do, which varies considerably by individual, by location, and by who is being detained.

Most ECA enforcement in the outer system is conducted by corporate security contractors rather than anything resembling a state police force. Corporate contractors operate under commercial contracts with liability frameworks that are easier to manage than uniform state actors when things go wrong publicly. So when an ECA contractor detains a Titan-born worker for three weeks without charge, and that worker later dies of complications, the liability chain is complex enough that accountability is difficult to assign.

In ECA-controlled zones, the quality of enforcement varies depending on which contractor holds the local contract, the oversight structure, and the extent to which the local population can push back. An Earther with proper documentation moving through an ECA checkpoint has a much different experience from a Titan-born worker with a methane drop tattoo. The Colonial Code permits this differential treatment. And the contractors apply it constantly.

AI and the Declaration

The Pro-Human AI Declaration is one of the ECA’s foundational governance documents: thirty-three rules organized across five categories covering human control, power concentration, the human experience, individual agency, and corporate accountability. It was adopted in the early decades of the ECA’s existence and has been refined continuously since. On Earth and in the inner system, it is substantially maintained. The ECA does enforce it imperfectly, but it does so genuinely: violations are investigated, companies are sanctioned, and the oversight mechanisms have enough institutional independence to catch problems that the corporations responsible would prefer to keep quiet.

In the outer system, the Declaration is maintained a bit more selectively. The provisions on human control, safety testing, and corporate accountability are largely kept intact. What gets quietly relaxed are the Human Agency and Liberty provisions: the rules governing data rights, psychological privacy, behavioral addiction, and the obligation of AI systems to empower rather than enfeeble their users. This means in practice, the ECA-managed AI infrastructure in the outer system collects more data, deploys more manipulative engagement patterns, and provides less transparency about its operations than the same systems would be permitted to do on Earth.

The outer system population knows that something is different, and the ghost webs exist partly because the communities that built them didn’t trust ECA-managed information infrastructure. The Declaration’s loosened provisions help explain why that distrust has a technical basis rather than just a political one. What most outer system residents can’t do is prove the specific violations, because the Declaration’s enforcement mechanisms are themselves administered by the ECA, and the ECA’s outer system oversight reports go to the same institutional bodies that look past these relaxed standards.

For Characters and GMs

ECA characters work best when they’re not villains who know they’re villains. The institutional culture genuinely believes it is doing necessary work. An ECA administrator who has spent a career managing food logistics for a planet that almost starved is not wrong to say that outer system resources matter. A military officer who has studied Rhea and believes a clean, fast strike is more humane than a protracted occupation is not obviously wrong either. The richest ECA characters are the ones who hold a genuine belief in what they’re doing and encounter specific facts - about Day Zero, about the Colonial Code, about what the contractors actually do - that their institutional framework doesn’t have any good answers for.

The Cryo Guilds — In Depth

The Guilds are notable not as a single organization. They are a loose confederation of various separate trade guilds that cover different parts of the hydrocarbon, ice, and life-support economy, each rooted in a distinct part of the Saturnian system. They collectively share a council structure and a rotating chair. But beyond that, they share very little: different politics, contracts, and relationships to the war.

What holds them together isn’t a general hierarchy, but reputation and mutual dependence. A guild that actively participates in the confederation’s actions builds credit with the others in the confederation. A guild that consistently refuses would lose standing. There is no enforcement of this; there is no mechanism to enforce it, but everyone acts accordingly. The chair can call a confederation action, and each guild would discuss internally and vote on whether to join. The chair spends most of its two-year rotation building the kind of relationship capital that makes those votes go the right way.

The Chair Position

The chair rotates between guilds by confederation vote roughly every two years, but in practice, it’s roughly rotating between the 5 major Guilds. The position has no formal authority to bind any guild to anything, but it does have the platform to call a meeting and the credibility to be heard when doing it. A chair that calls too many actions without sufficient cause burns through goodwill fast. A chair that never calls an action becomes irrelevant. The best at the job understand that it’s mostly about listening to all the factions.

Complete Chronology

Everything that happened. From the first Martian boot-print to the fleet massing at Enceladus. Dates marked in red are events Earth would prefer you not read about.

Era I - The Inner System Spreads (2035 ~ 2100)

The foundation of everything that came after. Before the outer system was even a dream, humanity was learning what it even meant to live off-world.

2035
The First Martian Colonies
Mars colonization truly begins - not as a permanent installation yet, but as a series of expanding, lengthening presences. Each mission stays a little longer. The infrastructure accumulates slowly, then faster. For the first time, humans genuinely aren't just visiting another world.
2047
First Martian Land Grants
Private-public partnerships begin offering long-term residency rights on Mars. This is the moment colonization becomes a property question as much as a survival question. Early terraforming begins simultaneously - orbital mirrors and greenhouse gas release from dedicated industrial facilities. The timescale is generations. Nobody alive will see the result.
2063

Mars gains limited self-rule after its infrastructure becomes genuinely self-sustaining. Earth corporations retain mineral rights. The Accords are celebrated as progress; later colonial movements will call them a template for controlled independence - autonomy that stops exactly where extraction begins.
2079

The Martian Ceres Program launches using Mars-built ion freighters - the first significant piece of infrastructure built off-Earth, by off-Earth residents, without Earth's direct fabrication. 2085
The Beltway Boom
Ceres becomes the great transit and refueling hub of the inner solar system. Vesta and Pallas develop automated resource transport networks. Private ventures begin running the numbers on the outer planets. The economics of the Jovian system start to look viable. The age of the true outer system is still thirty years away, but someone is already drawing maps.

The first human eyes on the deep outer system. Enceladus, Titan, Rhea - worlds being born as places people actually live.

2102
First Enceladus Outpost
Automated drones land on Enceladus and begin drilling into the subsurface ocean. Biochemistry experiments begin almost immediately. Manned expeditions begin shortly after to handle what automated systems can't.
2111
Ganymede, Callisto, and the Jupiter Colonization Wave
Jovian missions establish orbital shipyards and ice-farming colonies around Jupiter's major moons. Jupiter's radiation environment is brutal - colonies operate in deep shielded bunkers or magnetic-protected domes on Ganymede. These early Jovian colonies become the template for hostile-environment habitation that the Saturn system will refine over the next generation.
2117
Titan Colonized
The focus is immediately on subsurface tunnels and methane sea mining. The first hundred colonists are corporate employees on fixed contracts. The atmosphere is thick enough that pressure suits are simpler than vacuum suits - but the cold and chemistry are remorseless. Population: 100. Nobody's thinking about independence yet. Nobody's thinking about much beyond staying warm.
2125 ~ 2130

Titan reaches 10,000, then 50,000. Enceladus passes 6,000. Unlike the corporate-industrial pattern of Titan or the scientific focus of Enceladus, Rhea's colonization is softer from the start - agricultural, educational, civic. 2134

2140
Tethys Colonized

The event that cannot be unmade.

Rhea's self-rule, inspired by Martian syndicalism, matures into open anti-Earth federation. Revolutionaries broadcast declarations of sovereignty. Daily Life on Titan

Ground Level

Most of daily life on Titan has nothing to do with the war. People eat, work, sleep, argue about the news, maintain equipment, and keep the systems running. What makes it different from anywhere on Earth is the environment those things happen inside.

Food & Water

Closed ecological systems are the standard. Vertical aeroponic farms in sealed chambers produce most fresh protein and vegetable matter. Cultured meat is common and unremarkable - meat that's never had a nervous system, grown in bioreactors. The concept of an animal being killed for food is something most Titan-born learn about the way they learn about other Earth customs: abstractly, from footage, without any felt sense of it. Water is aggressively recycled. Wasting either is understood as taking from the loop - which means taking from everyone else in it.

Language

Standard is the shared language, but outer-system dialects have drifted significantly over three generations of isolation. Auto-translation exists but introduces errors. Long-term colonists use compressed phrases with heavy implied context; newcomers find conversations impossible to follow. Asking someone where their family is from is a politically loaded question - most families are refugees from somewhere. See Section 09 for the full treatment of language drift, cultural markers, and identity.

Communication

Internal mesh networks operate in real time within a habitat. Between habitats, light-speed lag applies. Messages to Earth take 71 to 85 minutes one-way. There's no real-time conversation with Earth, only exchanges. The TFS maintains ghost-webs - encrypted mesh networks that bypass ECA monitoring. Everyone serious uses them. The ECA knows about them and can't fully shut them down.

Clothing & Suits

Inside, clothing trends toward fitted, practical styles - loose fabrics can catch in machinery, and the low gravity makes flowing clothing look strange. Outside, heavy suits are mandatory. Colony suits are thick, insulated, pressure-rated, and carry their own air supply; emergency patches are standard kit. Status is sometimes expressed through the quality of your suit's heating system rather than any external decoration. E-textiles in medical-grade suits monitor vital signs continuously.

Health

Genetic screening is routine. Low gravity means bone density management via medication, exercise, and diet is a lifelong obligation - something everyone on Titan does the way people on Earth might take vitamins, except in that it's not optional. Radiation exposure from Saturn's magnetosphere is a background concern; long-haul spacers carry prophylactic implants as standard. Mental health infrastructure exists and is comprehensively overwhelmed - the Assembly's crisis lines have been running at capacity since Day Zero and haven't dropped below it since.

You don't think about the atmosphere. You don't think about the cold. You think about your air scrubbers and your food cycle and whether the transit tunnel to Ring 4 is even pressurized today. That's what home feels like out here.

— TFS orientation document, 2181

Culture & Identity

Who People Are

Three generations is long enough to diverge. The people of the Saturn system talk differently, believe different things, and mark their bodies with different signs than the people who sent them out here. Some of it is drift. Some of it is deliberate. A lot of it is what happens when you spend your whole life in a sealed environment where the wrong decision kills everyone in the corridor.

The Haze-Born Identity

People born on Titan are called Haze-born - a term that started as mild condescension from Earth immigrants and was reclaimed so completely that it's now a marker of pride. The Haze-born are the first generation of humans for whom Titan is simply home, with no reference point anywhere else. They've never breathed unprocessed air. They've never felt rain. They experience Earth footage - blue sky, horizon, the weight of a full-g environment - as alien, and often as slightly threatening. The colors are wrong. Everything is too bright and too open and there's nothing between you and the sky.

Haze-born culture is shaped by enclosure. Space is never wasted. Silence is comfortable in a way it isn't for people from larger environments. The hab isn't a limitation - it's the world. Social bonds form quickly and run deep because you are literally breathing the same recirculated air as the people around you, and everyone knows it. The communal ethic isn't ideological for most Haze-born; it's the practical logic of closed systems, internalized until it looks like personality. Wasting anything - water, food, air, someone's time - isn't inefficiency. It's taking from someone else.

Language Drift

Outer-system speech compresses. Implied context replaces explicit statement. Technical terminology from hab engineering and life-support has become everyday vocabulary - the words for thermal failure and pressure variance are the words people reach for when they talk about anything that's about to go wrong. The rhythm has shifted toward shorter clauses and faster exchanges, a communication style optimized for suits and corridors where being long-winded costs air and attention.

Auto-translation between Earth Standard and outer-system dialects exists and works for literal content. It consistently fails on register, tone, and implication. An Earther receiving a translated message from a Titan-born speaker gets the words but not the weight of them. Several common outer-system expressions have no Earth Standard equivalent because they describe experiences Earth speakers have never had: the specific quality of recycled air in an old hab section, the way a hull creak sounds different depending on whether it's thermal expansion or structural stress, the social meaning of which direction someone is facing when they deliver news. These concepts exist in the language because the people who use it needed words for them.

You can always tell an Earther by how much they say. Out here, if you're using that many words, either something is very wrong or you don't trust the person you're talking to.

— Hazeport transit worker, 2183

Rhea grief markers Families with Rhea connections often carry small personal markers - specific colors, items, phrases. These are deliberately subtle because Earth-aligned zones ban Rhea commemoration. Recognizable to anyone from the outer system. Invisible to most Earthers. A bonding signal between strangers who need to establish trust quickly.
Stripped suit lining Suit with e-textile monitoring physically removed - jagged seam lines inside the collar and sleeve where the substrate was cut out. Signals that the wearer is doing something they don't want recorded. Used by TFS operatives in sensitive operations. Also used by smugglers, journalists, and anyone else with reasons to be off the grid.

Religion in the Outer System

The demographics of belief shifted dramatically when humans left Earth. Religions tied to specific geography - pilgrimage sites, sacred land, community practice rooted in a particular place - largely didn't survive the transit. What did survive were the traditions flexible enough to travel without a fixed address.

On Earth, the major religions retain roughly their historical population proportions. In the outer system, the picture is completely different. The majority - nearly 60% of the non-Earth population - is unaffiliated: atheist, agnostic, or simply without connection to any organized religious tradition. This isn't hostility to belief; it's the natural result of selecting for people who were willing to leave everything behind, combined with the practical reality that religious institutions that couldn't adapt to the outer system's conditions simply didn't take root there.

Religion as Political Terrain

Religious communities in the outer system are often the most durable social infrastructure outside the guilds - tight-knit, organized, with internal communication networks that predate and outlast faction affiliations. The ECA monitors religious assembly in controlled zones the same way it monitors political assembly. The TFS includes religious community leaders in hab governance councils. Neither side is neutral about it. For characters, religious identity isn't just personal - it's a network, a set of obligations, and a political position whether they want it to be or not.

New Cultural Forms

Three generations of isolation, adaptation, and crisis have produced cultural forms that have no Earth equivalent and are still being named. A few that are present in 2184:

Loop ethics - the informal moral philosophy that's emerged from closed ecological system living. The core principle is that nothing leaves the system and nothing is truly discarded; every action cycles back. This has extended beyond resource management into a broader ethics of consequence and reciprocity. In its political form, it underlies the Kraken Doctrine. In its personal form, it shapes how Haze-born people think about debt, obligation, and what it means to harm someone who shares your air.

Rhea memory culture - a diffuse artistic and commemorative movement that uses Rhea as metaphor and mirror. Because direct commemoration is banned in ECA zones, the movement has developed an extensive vocabulary of indirection: colors, specific visual compositions, musical structures, and narrative forms that encode Rhea without naming it. Recognizing these codes is a literacy that separates the outer system's cultural insiders from everyone else. On Titan, some of it is becoming less coded and more direct, now that the Assembly controls the broadcast infrastructure.

Hull culture - the informal traditions that have grown up around hab maintenance work and exterior operations. People who do EVA work regularly develop specific rituals around suit-up procedures, specific verbal protocols before going outside, specific ways of acknowledging each other on return. These vary by hab and by crew, but the underlying structure is consistent: going outside is a liminal act, and the community marks it. Characters who've done serious EVA work carry these habits. They're usually the last people to die in a vacuum emergency.

Technology in Use

The Kit

Technology in the Saturn system is advanced by any historical measure, but it operates under real resource constraints and supply lines that Earth can cut. Not everything Earth has reaches the outer system. Some things the outer system has built that Earth hasn't bothered with - because Earth has never needed to be this self-sufficient. For character purposes, the useful question is always: who has access to this, who maintains it, and what happens when it fails?

ECA Tech Advantage

Earth has higher-bandwidth communications, near-instant orbital strike capability, and superior financial leverage. What it lacks is local knowledge, political legitimacy, and the genuine will of the people it's trying to govern. The TFS is outgunned but not outmaneuvered - yet.

Agriculture & Food Systems

The food cycle isn't a background detail on Titan. It's what the colony actually is. Six million people kept alive by a closed loop that can't afford a single broken link - and every part of that loop is contested territory between guilds, the Assembly, and the Consortium.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
Aeroponics A liquid solution with nutrients is misted into air chambers where plants are suspended - roots exposed to misted air rather than submerged in anything. Uses up to 90% less water than even efficient conventional hydroponics, and requires no growing medium to replace. The dominant food-growing method on Titan. Most hab farms are aeroponic. A single failed mist pump can kill an entire crop cycle. Aeroponic technicians are among the most respected - and most pressured - workers in any hab.
Agricultural Robotics Automated systems handle seeding, harvesting, pest control, soil analysis, and yield monitoring with minimal human oversight. Higher crop quality and lower production costs than manual labor alone. Every major farm cluster uses agri-robots. Malfunction cascades quickly - a sensor fault in one unit can cause over-irrigation across a whole chamber. The people who maintain these systems are among the most critical workers in any hab, and tend to know it.
Cultured Meat Meat grown in bioreactors from cell cultures - no animal is raised or slaughtered. Produced without artificial growth hormones or antibiotics. Compared to conventional meat: 99% lower land use, 82–96% lower water use, 78–96% lower greenhouse emissions, 7–45% lower energy use. Completely normal to anyone born on Titan. The concept of an animal being killed for food is abstract and faintly disturbing to most locals. Earth visitors who express disgust are regarded with confusion. Cultured meat from the vats is simply called meat. The distinction only matters to Earthers.
Closed Ecological Systems Fully sealed ecosystems where every waste product from one process becomes input for another. CO2 from respiration feeds plants; urine and feces cycle back into nutrient streams; nothing leaves the loop. The system must balance or it degrades. The entire colony is one. The awareness that the system is closed - that everything you eat, breathe, and excrete is recycled back to you - is background knowledge that shapes how Titan-born people think about resources, waste, and consequence. Wasting water or food isn't merely wasteful - it's an act of harm against everyone in the loop.
Vertical Farming Crops grown in vertically stacked layers in a controlled environment. Can cultivate many varieties simultaneously without shared soil plots. Used alongside aeroponics - the two are often combined in large farm installations. Major hab-farms are vertical by necessity - horizontal space is too valuable. Some larger domes incorporate vertical farms that double as atmospheric parks; the only places on Titan where a person can stand among growing things under something approximating open sky. These spaces have significant psychological importance and are fiercely protected in hab governance disputes.

Materials & Construction

The outer ring of material science on Titan is largely dictated by Titan itself. Hydrocarbons cover the surface in seas and dunes and fill the atmosphere. Polymers handle roles that metal and wood fill on Earth. The cold that would kill you outside becomes an engineering asset indoors. The materials below are the ones that show up in how the colony is actually built, maintained, and fought over.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
Aerogel Ultralight synthetic material made by replacing the liquid in a gel with gas - 99.8% air by volume. Extremely low density and thermal conductivity. Can be made transparent. Structurally strong due to dendritic microstructure despite being rigid and dry. Tends to shatter under sharp impact. The primary insulating material in hab walls, pressure suits, and the observation dome panels that give wealthier districts their views of the methane lakes. Transparent aerogel panels are a status marker - your hab section has them or it doesn't. An aerogel breach in a suit is a slow death sentence as thermal protection fails.
Bioplastic / PHB Plastic produced from renewable biomass. On Titan, methanotroph bacteria consume methane and produce Polyhydroxybutyrate (PHB) at roughly 60% of their body mass - meaning every 3–5 kg of methane yields 1 kg of usable bioplastic. Can be composted and cycled back into methane by anaerobic organisms, closing the loop. The backbone of local manufacturing. If it isn't metal or aerogel, it's almost certainly bioplastic. Furniture, piping, tool handles, hab partition panels, clothing components, containers - all of it. The production cycle is one of the cleanest closed loops on Titan: the atmosphere feeds the bacteria; the bacteria feed the colony. Because anaerobic organisms can convert PHB back into methane, the material can be cycled back into the environment when it's no longer needed.
Graphene Single-atom-thick carbon in a honeycomb lattice. Nearly 100 times stronger than the strongest steel at the same thickness. Excellent heat and electrical conductor. Transparent in thin layers. A semimetal with unusual electronic properties. Used in high-stress structural components, suit reinforcement layers, and advanced electronics. Graphene composites appear in hull plating for military and high-end commercial vessels. Expensive to produce at scale outside of dedicated facilities - which mostly exist closer to Earth.
Metal Foam Solid metal, typically aluminum, with gas-filled pores throughout. 75–95% empty space by volume. Lightweight, excellent at absorbing energy and damping sound. Used in orthopedics, structural panels, and heat exchangers. Found in hab wall construction as an impact-absorption layer behind the primary hull, and in prosthetic limbs - the porous structure encourages vascularization in prosthetic bone interfaces. Common in mining equipment as a vibration dampener. Titan-born workers who have lost limbs to industrial accidents often have metal foam integrated into their replacement prosthetics.
Superalloys High-performance alloys capable of operating at extreme fractions of their melting point. Excellent mechanical strength, resistance to thermal creep, corrosion, and oxidation. Primary applications in propulsion systems, turbines, and nuclear reactor heat exchangers. Every spacecraft engine nozzle, every reactor heat exchanger, and every geothermal tap relies on superalloy components. Supply is controlled almost entirely by Earth manufacturing. One of the TFS's acute strategic vulnerabilities - they can maintain existing superalloy components but can't yet fabricate replacements at scale. Every worn engine nozzle is a clock ticking down.
Magnetorheological Fluid Magnetic particles suspended in carrier oil. Under a magnetic field, particles align and the fluid instantly transitions from liquid to a near-solid viscoelastic state. Used in precision lens polishing, body armor, and structural shock dampeners. Found in high-end pressure suits as an adaptive armor layer - the suit stiffens on impact, distributing force across a wider area. Also used in hab connection joints and transit tunnel couplings to absorb the micro-vibrations of methane sea pressure. Some TFS militia units have field-modified suit components with magnetorheological armor layers.
Metamaterials Engineered materials with properties not found in nature - derived from structure rather than base chemistry. Arranged in repeating patterns at sub-wavelength scales. Applications include advanced antennas, absorbers, sound filtering, and radar cross-section reduction. Applications include antennas, absorbers, superlenses, radar cross-section reduction, and sound filtering. In practice, anything that needs to be hard to detect - drones, communication relays, structural panels in sensitive areas - is a candidate for metamaterial treatment. The technology exists; access to it is a function of who can fabricate the required structures.

Electronics & Computing

The ECA's most effective tools against the TFS aren't military - they're informational. Biometric surveillance, ambient monitoring, intercepted mesh traffic. The ghost webs exist because the colony learned to route around this. Electronics in the Saturn system is two parallel races: one to monitor, one to disappear.

The Pro-Human AI Declaration

AI development across human space is governed by the Pro-Human AI Declaration, which comprises 33 rules across 5 categories: human control of AI systems, prevention of power concentration, protection of the human experience, individual agency and liberty, and corporate accountability. On Earth and in the inner system, these are substantially maintained. In the outer systems, the ECA quietly relaxes the Human Agency and Liberty provisions as a population management tool, loosening rules on data rights, psychological privacy, and AI systems' obligation to empower rather than manipulate their users. The outer system population knows their AI behaves differently in ECA-managed infrastructure. The ghost webs exist partly for this reason. What they can't do is formally prove it, because the enforcement mechanisms are administered by the same institution that authorized the relaxed standards.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
E-Textiles Fabrics with embedded digital components - conductors, batteries, sensors, integrated circuits. Two classes: garments with classical electronics embedded, and textiles with electronics woven directly into the substrate. Can monitor heart rate, respiration, temperature, activity, and posture continuously. Can also track position and flag anomalous physiological states. Medical-grade e-textile suits are standard issue for miners, engineers, and anyone working in hazardous zones. The suit knows when you're stressed, injured, or dying, and it reports to a central system. The ECA has used mandatory e-textile reporting in controlled zones as a surveillance infrastructure since 2178. The TFS requires workers in sensitive operations to wear e-textile-stripped suits - ones with the monitoring substrate physically cut out. You can tell someone's clearance level by the scars on their suit lining.
Biometrics Body measurement systems for identification and health monitoring - fingerprints, face recognition, iris scanning, palm vein patterns, gait analysis, voice patterns, typing rhythm. Can detect physiological anomalies in real time when combined with e-textiles. Every hab access point uses biometric identification. The ECA's systems are sophisticated enough to flag gait changes that correlate with stress or concealment. TFS operatives who move between controlled and free zones sometimes undergo deliberate gait retraining to defeat behavioral biometrics. There is a small underground trade in biometric spoofing implants - illegal under both ECA and TFS codes for different reasons.
3D Integrated Circuits Semiconductor devices built by stacking silicon wafers vertically and connecting them so they behave as a single unit. Reduces power consumption, shrinks footprint, increases processing density. More complex to manufacture and generate significant internal heat. The computing cores of modern pressure suits, agri-robot control systems, and ghost web relay nodes all use 3D IC architecture. The heat generation problem in sealed hab environments means 3D IC systems require dedicated thermal management - a common point of failure in field equipment. A suit computer that runs too hot is a life-safety issue; most experienced workers know how to read the subtle warning signs before formal diagnostic alerts trigger.
Exascale Computing Systems capable of 10¹⁸ floating-point operations per second. Enables high-precision modeling of weather, climate, biological systems, and complex optimization problems. Approaches the processing capacity of the human brain at the neural level. Earth's orbital stations carry exascale systems for strategic modeling and communications routing. In the outer system, exascale capacity is scarce and heavily contested - whoever runs the most capable processing infrastructure has a significant intelligence advantage in fleet modeling, signals analysis, and resource optimization.
Ambient Intelligence Electronic environments that sense and respond to human presence - devices that work in concert to support daily activities without explicit commands. Embedded, personalized, adaptive. Recognizes individuals and their situational context. Wealthier hab districts have ambient intelligence infrastructure managing air, light, temperature, and resource allocation automatically. In poorer outer-ring districts, the same infrastructure exists but in degraded form - systems that technically function but whose personalization data is years out of date, or whose sensor arrays have half their nodes offline. The quality of your ambient environment is a direct index of your economic position. ECA-controlled zones use ambient intelligence for population monitoring; the system that adjusts your lighting also reports your movement patterns.
6G Cellular Networks Broadband cellular communication with speeds up to 1 terabit per second. Heterogeneous, decentralized architecture. Supports augmented reality, pervasive intelligence integration, and the Internet of Things at scale. The backbone of intra-hab communication. Earth's 6G nodes in the Saturn system have been progressively degraded since the blockade - some relay infrastructure was physically destroyed in the 2176 Enceladus sabotage. The TFS ghost webs run on improvised mesh nodes built from repurposed 6G components, deliberately fragmented to prevent single points of failure. Signal quality in outer-ring habs is poor; in the deeper Assembly-controlled tunnel networks, it's often intentionally suppressed to limit ECA intercept capability.
Twistronics Engineering the electrical properties of 2D materials by controlling the angle between stacked layers. Bilayer graphene twisted to specific angles can range from insulating to superconducting. Properties can be switched with minimal energy input. An emerging research area - the ability to switch a material from insulating to superconducting with minimal energy makes it valuable anywhere you need components that can disappear from the electromagnetic spectrum. A relay that draws no power and leaves no electrical signature is useful for communication networks that need to be hard to find.
Silicene Components Silicon analogue of graphene - single-atom-thick, but with a buckled rather than flat topology. Stronger interlayer coupling than graphene. Better bandgap tunability, enabling transistors that operate at room temperature. Chemically reactive surface that can be modified to alter electronic properties. Found in cutting-edge field processors and sensor arrays. Silicene is difficult to fabricate outside controlled laboratory conditions and highly sensitive to contamination - it's a prestige material in the outer system, associated with whoever has access to the right lab infrastructure. Its chemically reactive surface also means it can be modified to change its electronic properties, which has applications in encryption hardware.

Energy Systems

Fission is the only practical near-term power source at this distance from the sun. Titan's thick atmosphere and available water ice make reactor coolant easy to source and more efficient to use than on Earth. The cold outside makes the superconducting grid viable. What makes all of it strategically fragile is that Earth controls the fissile material supply - or did, until the blockade made that leverage mutual.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
Gen 4 Nuclear Reactors Fourth-generation fission reactors with improved safety, sustainability, and efficiency. Two subtypes: thermal reactors using moderated slow neutrons; fast reactors using unmoderated neutrons that can breed fuel or burn actinide waste. Can be configured to be fuel self-sustaining over time. Primary power for all major habitats and spacecraft. Fast reactors are particularly valuable in the outer system - given the difficulty of resupply, a reactor that gradually closes its own fuel cycle is strategically invaluable. The ECA controls the supply of fissile material for reactor fueling. This was one of the original economic leverage points that drove the TFS toward the Kraken Doctrine of self-sufficiency. Several TFS-aligned habs have quietly begun fuel-breeding programs.
Hydrocarbon Combustion Burning Titan's atmospheric and surface methane for heat and power. Abundant local fuel. Carbon emissions are not a concern in Titan's reducing atmosphere - the CO2 is chemically inert in context and cycles back into organic chemistry. The foundation of Titan's economy and a primary backup power source when nuclear systems are offline. Every hab has hydrocarbon combustion backup capacity. The fact that Titan's primary export is also its primary local fuel source is a permanent tension in trade negotiations - Earth wants the methane shipped out; Titan needs it to stay alive.
Airborne Wind Turbines Wind energy capture systems supported in the air without towers - kite or balloon based. Access higher-altitude winds with greater velocity and persistence than surface installations, without the material cost of tower construction. Deployed in Titan's upper atmospheric layer where wind speeds are consistent and high. A supplementary power source for surface installations and transit infrastructure. The TFS has begun deploying mobile airborne turbine arrays that can be repositioned quickly - making them harder for orbital surveillance to use as fixed infrastructure targets.
Geothermal Taps Extraction of thermal energy from Titan's internal heat, residual from formation and radioactive decay - requires drilling to significant depth but provides a stable, continuous power source independent of surface conditions or fuel supply. The most strategically secure power source available to the TFS - Earth can't interdict it. Major geothermal installations are deep enough that orbital strikes can't reach them without weapons that would also destroy significant hab infrastructure. The TFS has prioritized expanding geothermal capacity since the blockade began. The people who drill and maintain geothermal taps work in conditions of extreme isolation, heat, and pressure.
External Superconducting Grid Titan's extreme surface temperatures, typically around −179°C — bring many ceramic superconductor materials to near-viable operating thresholds. By routing the main electrical distribution grid outside the habitats rather than inside them, the colony exploits the ambient cold as a natural coolant, dramatically improving transmission efficiency. The cold that would kill a person in minutes becomes an engineering asset at grid scale. Most habs run their primary power distribution through exterior conduit runs embedded in the ice or mounted on the outer hull, kept near ambient temperature. Interior hab networks carry only the final distribution. This means that a major exterior damage event — whether from a methane weather system, a structural failure, or a deliberate strike - can simultaneously cut power to large sections of a hab. Grid engineers who understand the exterior routing are among the most critical technical personnel in the colony. They're also extremely aware that the grid is exposed in ways that interior systems are not.
Nuclear Fission Fuel Supply Fission remains the only fully practical high-density power source for the outer system at this technology level. Uranium's energy density is extraordinary - approximately 80 million megajoules per kilogram, or around 1.5 billion megajoules per liter. A few tons of fissile material, transportable on a single cargo run, can supply megawatts of power for centuries. Titan's thick atmosphere and available water ice make reactor coolant easy to source locally, and atmospheric cooling is more efficient here than on Earth. Earth controls the supply of processed fissile material to the outer system. This was always understood as a strategic lever - one of the last remaining dependencies that the TFS can't easily break. Since the blockade, new fuel deliveries have stopped entirely. The TFS is running existing reactor fuel inventories and extending fuel cycles where possible. A reactor that gradually breeds its own fuel from actinide waste is therefore worth more to them than almost any other single piece of infrastructure.

Medicine & Biology

Three generations of low gravity, closed-loop environments, and biological isolation have produced a population with medical needs that Earth's research base wasn't designed around. The medicine that works here was developed here - including the shift from antibiotics to phage therapy, which happened because closed colony environments accelerate bacterial resistance faster than anywhere on Earth.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
Artificial Uterus External gestation system that supports fetal development outside the parent's body. Originally developed for high-risk pregnancies; adopted as standard on Titan where low gravity makes conventional birth extremely dangerous - hemorrhage risk in 0.14g is severe, and the colony's early surgical infrastructure couldn't manage it safely. Every person born on Titan in the last forty years was born this way. It's not a medical procedure to locals - it's simply how birth works. Earth journalists who express discomfort or moral objection are regarded with genuine bafflement. The concept that this is unusual requires explanation. Titan-born characters have no visceral connection to the idea of gestation as an embodied experience.
Phage Therapy Treatment of bacterial infections using bacteriophages - viruses that target specific bacterial strains. A phage attaches to a bacterial cell, injects its genome, and hijacks the cell's replication machinery, stopping the infection. Highly selective; phage cocktails are used to improve coverage across multiple strains. As bacteria evolve resistance, phages can evolve countermeasures in response. Standard first-line treatment for bacterial infections across all Saturn system habs. Traditional broad-spectrum antibiotics became largely ineffective in closed-loop colony environments decades ago - bacterial resistance develops rapidly when the same population is repeatedly exposed to the same drugs. Phage therapy replaced them. Characters who grew up elsewhere and received antibiotic-based treatment as children may carry resistant bacterial populations that phage cocktails on Titan aren't calibrated for.
Enzybiotics Enzyme-based antimicrobials derived from bacteriophage proteins that can destroy bacterial cell walls directly, without relying on the phage's full infection cycle. Faster acting than phage therapy in some applications. Growing concern that bacteria will develop resistance to these as well - an arms race with no guaranteed end state. Used alongside phage therapy as a second line of treatment or in cases where phage targeting is uncertain. The researchers developing new enzybiotics at Titan's biological institutes are in a continuous race against bacterial adaptation. Some of them have been working the same problem for twenty years. There's a quiet anxiety in the medical community that the arms race may eventually be one they lose.
Stem Cell Treatments Regenerative therapy using undifferentiated stem cells that can develop into any tissue type. Treats stroke damage, traumatic brain injury, spinal cord injuries, bone marrow failure, wound healing, and degenerative conditions. Used in everything from joint repair after industrial accidents to treatment of the neurological effects of long-term low-gravity exposure. Widely available and commonly used - industrial accident rates in mining and construction make regenerative medicine a constant necessity. Workers who have had stem cell joint reconstruction, spinal repair, or organ tissue regeneration are not unusual. The treatments are routine but not instantaneous - recovery takes weeks, and during that time a worker is economically vulnerable. Guild medical coverage is a key labor negotiation point.
Genetic Engineering Direct manipulation of an organism's genome using biotechnology. Includes gene therapy to correct hereditary conditions, modification of organisms for industrial or agricultural use, and - more controversially - germline editing that passes modifications to offspring. Can produce improved crop resistance, engineered bacteria for industrial processes, and targeted cures for genetic disease. Medical genetic engineering is standard and uncontroversial for treating heritable conditions. Agricultural genetic engineering of crop strains is routine. Germline modification - changes that pass to children - is technically regulated under both ECA and TFS codes, but enforcement in the outer system has been inconsistent for decades. Some Titan-born families carry modifications made two or three generations back. The long-term physiological effects of generational low-gravity combined with untracked germline changes are a growing concern in the medical community - a source of genuine uncertainty about what Titan-born bodies will look like in another generation.
Tissue Engineering Growing biological tissues and organs outside the body using scaffolds, cell cultures, and growth factors. Applications include organ replacement, lab-grown cartilage and bone, skin grafts, and bioartificial hearts. The same technology that underlies cultured meat production. Organ replacement waiting lists don't exist on Titan - if you need a new kidney, it's grown from your own cell line in a matter of weeks. Access is theoretically equal but practically tiered - faster fabrication and better outcomes correlate with economic position and guild affiliation, as they do with everything else.
Body Implants & Prosthetics A spectrum from functional medical replacements to augmentative modifications. Medical: limb replacements with metal foam bone interfaces that encourage vascularization, spinal stabilizers, radiation inhibitors, cochlear and visual augmentation. Augmentive: neural interface chips for technology control, enhanced sensory implants, subdermal biometric transponders. Most militarized augmentations are regulated but not consistently enforced. Industrial accident prosthetics are common and normalized - losing a hand or forearm in mining or construction and receiving a functional replacement is an unremarkable life event to outer-system residents. Radiation inhibitor implants are standard for anyone who spends significant time in transit. The dividing line between medical necessity and augmentation is culturally negotiated rather than biologically fixed - what counts as a treatment versus an enhancement depends on who is paying and who is asking. ECA agents and some TFS special operations units carry implants that would be illegal under normal codes.
Personalized Medicine Medical treatment tailored to an individual's genetic profile, predicted drug responses, and specific risk factors. Requires detailed genomic sequencing and significant computational resources. More effective than standardized treatments but substantially more expensive to deliver. Available to anyone in the Saturn system, but the computational cost creates a tiered system in practice - full personalized medicine workups are a Consortium perk, a guild benefit for senior members, or an Assembly priority allocation for critical personnel. Standard citizens get population-averaged treatment protocols. The difference in outcomes is real and measurable. The difference in outcomes between personalized and standard treatment is real and measurable. Having a full genomic profile on file is both a medical advantage and a data security question.

Communications & Information

68 to 84 minutes one-way to Earth. That lag is the single most defining fact of outer-system politics - there's no real-time conversation with Earth, only exchanges, and every instruction from Earth arrives already out of date. The TFS built its ghost webs specifically because the ECA controls the official relay infrastructure. Communications here is a war that's been running longer than the military one.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
Ghost Webs / Mesh Networks Decentralized encrypted communication networks with no central node. Each relay both receives and retransmits, so destroying any single point doesn't break the network. Designed to be self-healing - losing 30% of nodes causes degraded but functional performance. The nervous system of the TFS. Built over a decade from repurposed 6G relay hardware and improvised encryption layers. ECA signals intelligence can detect traffic patterns but cannot reliably decrypt content or identify senders. Operating a ghost web node is a political act - the ECA classifies it as seditious infrastructure. Most active TFS members have a node somewhere: in a wall panel, inside a piece of industrial equipment, in the lining of a bag.
Slow-Laser Communications High-bandwidth directional laser communication for long-distance point-to-point data transmission. "Slow" because light-speed delay at interplanetary distances is unavoidable - 68 to 84 minutes one-way to Earth. Cannot be intercepted without being in the direct beam path, making it more secure than radio. The primary method for official communications between the Saturn system and Earth. The ECA controls the major laser relay infrastructure. Since the 2176 sabotage of Enceladus relay nodes, communication has been degraded and intermittent. The TFS has its own laser communication capability for intra-system use - Saturn's moons are close enough that lag is measured in minutes, not hours, and the TFS has invested heavily in directional laser links that can't easily be intercepted.
Ambient Intelligence Infrastructure Networked sensor and response systems embedded throughout hab environments. Manages air, temperature, lighting, access control, and resource allocation. When fully functional, the environment responds to presence and need without explicit instruction. When repurposed, it becomes a total surveillance apparatus. Both a convenience and a weapon. ECA-managed hab zones have ambient intelligence that logs movement, behavioral patterns, and physiological responses continuously. TFS engineers have developed techniques for "blinding" specific nodes without triggering malfunction alerts - leaving gaps in coverage that look like sensor noise rather than deliberate interference. Knowing which ambient sensors are blind, and when, is tactically valuable information that certain people sell.

Spacecraft & Propulsion

Most TFS vessels weren't built as spacecraft - they were converted. Methane tankers, ore freighters, passenger transports. The propulsion systems below are what those vessels run on, what Earth-built warships run on, and what the strategic difference between them actually looks like in practice.

Technology What It Does On the Ground
Plasma Propulsion Electric propulsion generating thrust from a quasi-neutral plasma — unlike ion thrusters, lacks acceleration grids that degrade over time. Can use a wide variety of propellants including argon, CO2 mixtures, and organic compounds. High efficiency but energy-intensive, limiting practical use to inner solar system ranges without significant power infrastructure. Standard propulsion for civilian transit vessels between Saturn's moons. The TFS's newly constructed combat craft use plasma drives — the variety of possible propellants means they can theoretically run on processed Titan atmosphere in an emergency. ECA fleet vessels use more powerful but less flexible systems. The TFS advantages in propellant flexibility may be tactically significant in a blockade environment.
Pulse Detonation Engines Propulsion using detonation waves rather than deflagration to combust fuel-oxidizer mixtures. Thermodynamically more efficient than conventional turbojets. Fewer moving parts - no compressor spools required. Operates from subsonic to roughly Mach 5 in atmosphere; functional in vacuum. Pulsed cycle requires mixture renewal between detonation events. Used in Titan's atmospheric transit craft - the thick nitrogen atmosphere makes air-breathing propulsion viable in ways impossible on most other worlds. Some TFS attack submarines operating in the methane seas use pulse detonation variants adapted for liquid-medium operation. The distinctive rhythmic sound of a pulse detonation engine - a rapid series of concussions rather than a continuous roar - is immediately recognizable to anyone who has worked near Titan's transit infrastructure.
Inflatable Hab Modules Pressurized expandable structures that increase internal volume after deployment. Cheaper to transport than rigid modules at equivalent internal volume. Used extensively in early colonization phases and in resource-constrained contexts where rigid construction is impractical. Less common on Titan than on lower-pressure worlds — Titan's thick, dense atmosphere makes inflatable structures harder to pressure-seal reliably and more vulnerable to slow leaks. More common on Mimas and in transit vessels as emergency auxiliary modules. Some of the oldest and poorest hab sections in outer-ring Hazeport are built around inflatable cores that were never replaced with rigid construction. The people who live in them know which panels flex in high-pressure events and which ones might not hold.
Swarm Robotics Coordination of large numbers of simple robots working collectively. Individual units are inexpensive and expendable; collective behavior produces complex emergent capabilities. Scalable by design. Applications in mining, search and rescue, material transport, and - increasingly - military reconnaissance and sabotage. Standard for hull inspection and breach response on large vessels and hab exteriors. Mining operations use swarm units for tunnel boring and ore transport in spaces too small for human-piloted equipment. The TFS has adapted commercial swarm units for military use: sensor sweeps, relay node deployment in hostile territory, and disruption of ECA drone patrols. A cloud of small robots is significantly harder for point defense systems to track and engage than a single larger target. The ECA has noted this adaptation and is developing countermeasures.
For Character Creation

Technology shapes identity out here. What your character works with every day - what they maintain, what they depend on, what they fear failing - is as defining as their faction or their history. A geothermal tap engineer has a different relationship to power and survival than a biometric systems technician. An aeroponic farmer thinks differently about waste and consequence than a cultured-meat vat operator. Ask not just what technologies your character uses, but which ones they understand deeply enough to improvise with when they break.

Conflict & Combat

How Violence Works

Violence in this setting isn't clean or heroic. It's dangerous, constrained by environment, and carries real political and resource costs. A firefight in a hab corridor is also a firefight inside a pressurized structure with controlled atmosphere, where a stray shot can puncture a wall and kill people who weren't involved. That's the frame everything here sits inside.

The Political Shape of Conflict in 2184

The war between the TFS and the ECA isn't symmetrical. Earth has orbital superiority, a professional military, and manufacturing capacity that the Saturn system can't match. The TFS has local knowledge, genuine popular support, terrain familiarity, and the strategic advantage of being nearly impossible to defeat without inflicting casualties that would create another Rhea - which the ECA understands it can't afford politically.

This creates a specific kind of stalemate. The ECA can't simply destroy Titan without destroying the resource infrastructure it was sent to secure. The TFS can't beat Earth in open naval engagement. So neither side is fighting to win outright. The ECA is fighting to make the cost of continued resistance unbearable. The TFS is fighting to make the cost of occupation exceed whatever Titan is worth. Both sides understand this perfectly. The question is which calculation breaks first.

The Rhea Constraint

The ECA's greatest tactical capability - mass orbital bombardment - is its most politically constrained option. Every use of it risks creating another Rhea: a documented atrocity that radicalizes the entire outer system and hands the TFS a generation of recruits and sympathizers. ECA hardliners argue that a decisive strike ends the war quickly. Moderates argue that it guarantees a war that never ends. This internal division is one of the TFS's most important strategic assets.

Levels of Conflict

Most of what characters experience isn't a pitched battle. The war in 2184 is mostly friction - the accumulated weight of checkpoints, shortages, intercepted messages, and decisions made under pressure. The table below covers the full range from daily life to open engagement.

Ground & Hab Combat

Fighting inside a habitat is fighting inside a life support system. The walls keep you alive. The air mix is controlled. The pressure differential between inside and outside is significant. Every tactical decision carries the knowledge that the wrong choice doesn't just get people killed - it can kill everyone in the hab section.

Titan's surface gravity is 0.14g. This is low enough to fundamentally change how bodies move, how force transfers, and what weapons are viable, but not so low that movement is impossible. You're not floating. You're walking on a world where everything weighs a seventh of what you expect, where a stumble sends you two meters sideways, and where jumping incorrectly can put you into an uncontrolled bounce off a ceiling.

Long-term residents have internalized this. Their movement is adapted - economical, grounded, never wasted. Newcomers from higher-gravity environments spend weeks relearning how to walk. In combat, this adaptation difference matters enormously. A Titan-born fighter knows exactly how much force to apply, how to convert a surface into leverage, how to arrest their own momentum without a surface in reach. An Earth-born soldier, however physically superior in raw strength, is constantly fighting their own instincts.

The Physics of Fighting Here

[01]
Anchor Before Anything
Magnetic boot soles, gecko-grip gloves, a hand on a railing, a foot hooked under a conduit. Without an anchor you have no leverage and no reaction mass. The physics equation is F=ma - if you want your force to go into your opponent, you need the mass of the entire structure behind you. Unanchored, you are a seventh of your Earth weight. Anchored, you have the station.
[02]
Grapple Is King
Once two people are locked together, neither goes flying alone. Grappling styles evolve significantly in low-g — takedowns function differently, leverage points shift, ground positions are meaningless — but the fundamental principle holds. Getting hold of someone is the first objective. Keeping hold is the second. Everything else follows from that.
[03]
Never Punch Hard
A full-force punch in 0.14g launches you backward nearly as fast as it sends your opponent forward. Strikes work in low-g only when you have solid anchorage and a compact delivery - short, controlled, using the body's mass rather than a swing. Wire kung-fu fight choreography is closer to the reality than boxing. The hardest hit is the one delivered while perfectly grounded.
[04]
Use Momentum as a Weapon
Objects in motion stay in motion. Throwing an opponent into free-float - away from any surface - removes them from the fight entirely until they reach the next wall. Using your own velocity as impact force - springing from a surface and driving into someone like a projectile - can be decisive. A moving body in low-g doesn't slow down. The next wall is wherever the math says it is.
[05]
Avoid Airtime
Becoming airborne - not by choice, not with a surface immediately reachable - is tactical death. You can't change direction. You can't fire accurately. If you launched with a spin, you can't stop it without an anchor. You are a predictable trajectory. Every experienced fighter in a hab environment treats getting launched off a surface as the primary thing to prevent, not just a setback to recover from.
[06]
Think in Spheres, Not Planes
Earth combat doctrine is built on a two-dimensional surface with a dominant up/down. In a hab corridor or open interior space, threat can come from any direction — including directly above and below. Defensive formations adapt: covering angles in three dimensions, positioning so that fire lanes do not cross through teammates, understanding that the ceiling is as usable as the floor.

Weapons in Hab Environments

The choice of weapon is environmental before it's tactical. The wrong weapon in the wrong hab doesn't just fail to work - it can breach a wall, ignite an atmosphere, or contaminate a pressurized space. These constraints are what actually shape how people fight here, not preference.

Weapon Type Why It Works Here Why It Doesn't
Short Blades (knives, short swords) Ideal for grapple range - can be used one-handed while the other maintains grip. Can penetrate gaps in pressure suit fabric and armor joints. No recoil. No atmosphere contamination. The dominant close-quarters weapon in hab interiors. Limited reach. Requires commitment to close range. Against an anchored opponent with a longer reach weapon, you need superior grappling to close the distance safely.
Guisarme / Reach Hook (EVA environments) In vacuum or open exterior environments, reach is decisive. A hooked polearm can snag an opponent's suit, drag them off a surface, puncture suit fabric - any small tear is a countdown. Doubles as a mobility tool for catching handholds when dislodged. The thin hook point is designed for suit puncture, not deep wounds. Too long for corridor work. Dangerous to swing near hab walls and pressure infrastructure. In a grapple, a long weapon becomes an encumbrance rather than an advantage.
Recoilless Rifle Vents propellant gas rearward through an open back, neutralizing recoil. Caseless ammunition eliminates ejected brass as a projectile hazard. Can use slow, heavy slugs designed to penetrate flesh and suit fabric without punching through hab walls. The standard ranged weapon for hab security forces. Less energy-efficient than sealed-breech firearms - requires a larger round to achieve the same stopping power. The vent blast rearward is dangerous to anyone standing directly behind the shooter. Reload is more complex in a grapple.
Blowdart / Pneumatic Launcher Propulsion comes from gas pressure, not explosive charge - zero recoil. Short pipes are viable in low-g where conventional accuracy is already degraded. Darts with pharmacological payloads are tactically useful for non-lethal incapacitation in hab environments where lethal force may compromise the structure. A weapon that requires no electronic components, no ammunition supply chain, and no maintenance. Short range. Low penetrating power against armored opponents. Requires deliberate aim which is complicated by the three-dimensional threat environment.
Beam Weapons (lasers, microwave emitters) Zero recoil. Speed-of-light accuracy - impossible to dodge once fired. Effective at airtime targets. In high-enough power configurations, can disable suit systems or compromise structural integrity without the blowthrough risk of projectiles. The ECA's enforcement units carry directed-energy sidearms for close-quarters use. Expensive. High power draw. Generate significant heat in the shooter's equipment. In a grapple, useless. In an oxygen-enriched atmosphere, a sufficiently high-power beam can ignite the air mixture. Using one carelessly near a life-support conduit can start a fire that kills the entire hab section.
Whips / Hooked Flexible Weapons Can redirect an opponent's momentum rather than opposing it directly — useful when you cannot anchor firmly enough to absorb an impact. A hooked whip can pull someone out of airtime and into a controlled position, or launch them in a chosen direction. Useful for herding opponents away from defensible positions. Requires significant skill to use effectively in three dimensions. In crowded environments, the swing radius is a hazard to allies. Against a grappler who gets inside the weapon's range, the user is at a severe disadvantage.

EVA and Vacuum Combat

Fighting outside - on the exterior hull of a hab or vessel, or in open vacuum - operates on different rules entirely. There's no contained atmosphere to protect. There's no wall to push off of unless you're already near one. And a suit breach isn't an injury. It's a countdown - the kind where you can feel the temperature dropping and you know exactly how much time you have left.

Suit puncture in vacuum is the primary lethality mechanism. A small hole - the kind a hooked guisarme point is designed to make - causes gradual pressure loss. Suit emergency sealant can slow it. A large tear can't be sealed in the field. Ranged weapons are preferred where available because they don't produce the indefinitely-traveling ricochets that a missed projectile does in vacuum. Beam weapons even more so - no ricochet problem at all, and they don't leave holes in the hull.

Maneuvering in open vacuum requires an AMU - an Astronaut Maneuvering Unit, essentially a thruster pack. Without one, you are whatever trajectory your last contact with a surface gave you. With one, you have limited three-dimensional mobility, though the fuel supply constrains how aggressively you can maneuver. AMU combat is a fuel-budget problem as much as a combat problem. Wasting thrust on dramatic maneuvers means being helpless when it matters.

Enforcement, Detention, and Law

Most conflict characters experience isn't military. It's the friction of living in a contested space - checkpoints, inspections, detention, and the constant low-level negotiation between what is permitted and what is necessary.

In ECA-controlled zones, enforcement is conducted by corporate security contractors and orbital command personnel. The legal framework is Earth Colonial Code, which gives enormous discretionary authority to enforcement personnel and provides minimal procedural protections. Detention without charge is standard. The practical floor of enforcement is "whatever the contractor decides," which varies significantly by individual, by hab, and by who is being detained. An Earther with documentation is treated differently from a Titan-born worker with a methane drop tattoo.

In TFS-controlled zones, enforcement is conducted by Assembly militia - a mix of former Cryo Guild security teams, volunteers, and personnel who were doing this work informally before the Assembly formalized it. TFS law is still being written and is inconsistently applied. The Assembly is genuinely trying to build legitimate governance under active siege conditions; the result is often functional but rarely elegant. Some militia commanders are principled and careful. Others are running personal fiefdoms under an Assembly flag.

Combat Tone Note

Violence here should feel constrained and consequential. In hab interiors: slow, careful, physical - like wrestling in a space where the floor might be the ceiling and the wrong move cracks a wall. In EVA: fast, quiet, and final. In spacecraft engagement: clinical and impersonal at distance, terrifying when it gets close. The setting doesn't reward dramatic heroics - it rewards preparation, knowledge of the environment, and the restraint to know when not to escalate.

Spacecraft in the Saturn System

Ships & the Void

Between Saturn's moons, journeys take days to weeks. A ship is where you live for that time - a sealed, pressurized environment that you share with a small number of people, cut off from everything else. The physical design follows from the physics. The culture follows from the confinement. Both matter for characters who spend any time off Titan.

What Ships Actually Look Like

Forget the science fiction imagery of flat, swept-wing spacecraft or disc-shaped cruisers. Ships in this setting are built along the thrust axis - tall and narrow, like skyscrapers laid on their side, or standing vertical when under thrust. The engine is at one end. Everything else stacks toward the other end along the spine. When the engines fire, the thrust runs through the structure from bottom to top, and the crew experiences the thrust as gravity pointing toward the engine end - their floor is the engine-ward bulkhead.

When engines aren't firing - during coasting phases that can last days or weeks between burns - there's no gravity. Crew live in microgravity, anchored by magnetic soles and handholds, sleeping in tethered bunks, eating food that stays in containers. Some larger vessels use spin gravity - a rotating section of the hull providing centrifugal force that simulates a fraction of a g. It's expensive in mass and complexity, and the Coriolis effect makes it disorienting for newcomers. Most working vessels don't bother.

The Expanse Comparison

The ship aesthetic here is explicitly closer to The Expanse than to Star Wars or Star Trek. No horizontal corridors where the floor is down regardless of thrust. No artificial gravity that works like magic. Thrust is gravity. Coasting is weightlessness. The orientation of everything aboard - furniture, displays, equipment mounts - is designed around a specific thrust direction, and when that direction changes, the whole ship reorients.

The Three Basic Ship Types

Not all ships are warships, and most people who travel in space never board one. The three categories below cover most of what characters will encounter.

Cycler / Freighter Long-haul cargo and passenger transport between planetary systems and between Saturn's moons. Designed for endurance over performance - maximum delta-v, minimal crew overhead, large cargo volume. Journeys measured in weeks to months. Small professional crews, often guild-affiliated. Senior pilots, engineers, cargo specialists. Often family operations on smaller vessels - multiple generations working the same ship. Monotonous, close-quarters, and intimate. Crew of six sharing a vessel for three months develop either deep bonds or genuine hatred. The ship becomes the whole world. Mail, delayed news from home, rationed entertainment. Some crews maintain elaborate hab culture - gardens in the hydroponics module, carefully maintained personal spaces, rituals around departure and arrival.
Patrol / Corvette Short-to-medium range armed vessel for system patrol, interdiction, blockade enforcement, and rapid response. Faster and more maneuverable than freighters. Carry weapons, point defense systems, and boarding capability. ECA uses them for blockade; TFS is building them as fast as it can from converted civilian hulls. Military or paramilitary crews. ECA Orbital Command personnel. TFS Assembly militia with naval training. Sometimes mercenary contractors working for either side or neither. Higher intensity than freighter life - the possibility of engagement is always present. Drills are constant. Crew relationships are forged under stress. A TFS corvette crew is often ideologically unified in a way that ECA crews are not - the ECA is a job; the TFS is a cause. This difference in motivation has tactical implications.
Drone Carrier / Capital Ship Large, heavily armed vessels designed to project force over a wide area. Carry drone complements, missiles, railguns, and point defense networks. Expensive in mass, crew, and reactor capacity. The ECA's Black Tides Fleet consists primarily of capital-class vessels; the TFS has nothing comparable. Conflict between departments over resources and priorities is constant and managed. Long deployments create distinct shipboard cultures - traditions, slang, rituals - that outsiders find opaque.

What Ships Are Made Of

The four systems below are what every ship runs on. In combat, they're also the priority target list - in rough order of what an enemy gunner is trying to hit first.

Reactors generate power. The larger the ship, the more reactors it carries - and reactors produce radiation, so they're placed with careful thought about where crew compartments are. Fuel tanks make excellent radiation shields and are often positioned between reactor sections and inhabited areas. A reactor running at high output generates significant heat, which must be expelled.

Radiators expel that heat into space - the only way to cool a vessel in vacuum. They're large, lightly armored, and structurally exposed. In combat, they're almost always the first thing destroyed, because a ship that can't dump heat eventually cooks its own systems. A warship whose radiators are gone has minutes to hours before it must reduce power output to survivable levels or destroy itself from within. Experienced gunners target radiators before weapons, because a blind ship can still fire back.

Engines provide thrust. Nozzles are exposed and relatively fragile. Disabling an enemy's propulsion doesn't kill the crew - it leaves them adrift, unable to maneuver, at the mercy of whoever controls the engagement. This is often more useful than destruction. A disabled ship is a captured ship, a propaganda asset, and a source of salvage.

Fuel tanks make up most of the internal volume of most vessels. Delta-v - the total change in velocity a ship can achieve - is the fundamental currency of space travel. Every maneuver, every course correction, every combat burn costs delta-v. Ships are designed around their fuel budget. A vessel that runs low on propellant in the outer system can't simply find a filling station. Fuel logistics is one of the ECA's most significant advantages in the current conflict - the blockade is partly about cutting Titan's access to propellant resupply.

Weapons and What They Mean in Practice

Space weapons aren't like ground weapons. Ranges are measured in hundreds of kilometers. Engagements last minutes to hours. The physics involved are unforgiving and the margins for error are small. What follows is how weapons are actually experienced by the people near them.

Weapon Effective Range What It's Like to Use / Face
Pulse Laser (PLA) Longest - effectively unlimited, but power drops sharply with distance due to diffraction You can't dodge a laser - it travels at the speed of light and you'll never see it coming. What you experience is the damage after the fact, or the heat warnings before it, or the display showing your radiator array is being cooked. At long range, lasers are a precision tool for disabling exposed systems — melting sensor arrays, punching holes in nozzles, burning out radiator panels. Operators sit at a console. It feels more like surgery than combat. The terrifying version is close range - at boarding distance, a powerful beam cuts through hull plating and people alike.
Missiles Theoretically unlimited - but fuel-constrained and increasingly effective countermeasures reduce practical range A missile launch is a decision that can't be undone. Once it's away, it's on a vector toward something. The crew watches the threat track appear on their displays - a contact, accelerating, closing. Point defense begins engaging. Some missiles die in the fire. Others don't. The interval between launch detection and impact is measured in minutes, sometimes less. It's one of the longer intervals of focused dread in combat. Being on the firing end, watching your missile track toward a target, watching their point defense spin up - it's clinical until it isn't.
Railgun Long - limited by targeting accuracy and power availability, not the round itself A railgun slug is a dense metal rod traveling at several kilometers per second. It has no propellant, no guidance, no signature after leaving the barrel. At combat ranges, the time between firing and impact is seconds. No warning.. The ship just takes the hit - a hole punched through whatever was in the path, energy depositing catastrophically into whatever it encounters. Railguns are spinal-mounted, meaning the entire ship must turn to aim. The rotation is slow and obvious. Everyone on both sides knows what it means when a ship begins to rotate.
Drones Variable - deployed to extend sensor range, create debris fields, or serve as sacrificial decoys Drones are not primarily killers in the current conflict - they are the eyes. A drone net deployed around a transit corridor gives TFS forces early warning of ECA approach vectors hours before direct sensor contact. Drones creating debris fields ahead of an advancing fleet are a passive weapon - the debris doesn't kill ships, but it saturates point defense and sensor systems, degrading response capability. The ECA's drone advantage is one of the TFS's most acute problems; they are effectively blind in any region the ECA chooses to saturate.
Point Defense Cannons (PDCs) Short - designed specifically for engaging incoming missiles and drones at close range PDCs are the heartbeat of a warship under attack. They fire continuously in engagement, tracking and destroying incoming ordnance automatically - operators set parameters and watch. The sound aboard a ship running PDCs is a constant mechanical rhythm, hundreds of rounds per second, the ship shuddering with the effort of it. When the PDCs go quiet, either the attack is over or the PDCs are gone. Neither feels safe. A PDC operator's job is to watch the saturation count - the number of simultaneous incoming contacts - and know when the math stops being in their favor.
Grapplers (Boarding Pods) Intimate - for hull contact and forced entry Boarding is the most personal form of space combat. A grappler pod attaches to an enemy hull, burns or cuts through, and delivers people into the interior of a vessel that is doing everything it can to stop them. The crew on the receiving end hears the contact - a thud against the hull, then cutting sounds, then the hiss of pressure equalization as a breach is made. For the boarding team, the first few meters of hostile corridor are the most dangerous part of the operation. Everyone on both sides is in a pressurized container. Nobody wants to breach a wall.

The TFS Fleet - What They Actually Have

The TFS did not build a navy. It converted, improvised, salvaged, and in a few cases stole one. Understanding the TFS fleet is understanding a military built by engineers who are better at problem-solving than doctrine.

Most TFS vessels started as something else - methane tankers with weapons mounts welded to the spine, ore freighters with point defense arrays jury-rigged into the cargo bay, passenger transports with their civilian transponders stripped and their crew modules packed with militia. They're slower than ECA warships, less armored, and individually outclassed in almost every metric. What they have instead: their crews know Titan's orbital environment in the way you only know a place you've lived in for years, and they're fighting for something rather than being paid to fight for something. That difference shows up in engagement.

The plasma rail platforms beneath Kraken Mare are the TFS's most strategically significant military asset - fixed installations that ECA orbital surveillance can't directly target without risking collateral damage to the hab infrastructure directly above them. They're not mobile. They're not versatile. But they can put a rail slug through an ECA vessel making a low approach over Titan's methane seas, and they can do it from below the surface, with no visible signature until the moment of firing.

The ECA Black Tides Fleet

The Black Tides Fleet is a purpose-built invasion force, staged at Enceladus and prepared for a full military action against Titan. It's the largest ECA force ever deployed in the outer system. It has professional crews, coordinated doctrine, drone superiority, and weapons systems that outrange anything the TFS can field. It also has a political problem: it can't simply destroy Titan. The resources it's been sent to secure are the same resources it would destroy in any total bombardment.

The fleet's command is divided between a hardline admiral who believes a fast, overwhelming strike is the only solution, and a political commissar attached by the ECA civilian authority who has the authority to countermand any order that risks creating another Rhea. This internal tension is known within ECA intelligence circles. It's also known within the Aurora Compact. Whether it's known within the TFS depends on how good their intelligence is - which depends on what your character knows and who they talk to.

For Character Creation

Ships are communities. A character who's spent years on a cycler has a completely different relationship to space - to confinement, to small crews, to the rhythms of transit - than one who's only ever been in a hab. A character who served on an ECA patrol corvette before defecting carries a very specific institutional knowledge. Ask what ships your character has been on, what happened aboard them, and who they shared a hull with for weeks at a time. The ship is often where the most formative relationships in a spacefarer's life were made.

Making Characters

Roleplay Guide

Characters here aren't defined by their powers or stats. They're defined by where they came from, what they lost, and what they want in 2184. Work through the questions below - you don't need answers before you start, but they'll pull you toward something real.

Where Are You From?

  • Were you born on Titan, or did you come from elsewhere? If you emigrated, why? What did you leave behind?
  • Which habitat? Hazeport's inner rings or outer sprawl? A smaller settlement near Kraken Mare? A mining outpost?
  • If you have any Rhea connection - survivor family, Rhea-born parent - what does that mean to you personally?
  • Have you ever been off-moon? What was that like?
  • Does your family hold any guild affiliation, or work outside the guild system?

What Do You Do?

  • What is your actual job? Who pays you, and what do they get for it?
  • Are you skilled in something that makes you useful to multiple factions - translation, engineering, medicine, combat?
  • Do you work with your hands, with data, with people, or with machines?
  • Have you ever crossed a faction line for money, safety, or conviction?
  • What is the most morally complicated thing your work has required of you?

Where Do You Stand?

  • Are you actively aligned with the TFS, the ECA, a guild, the Aurora Compact, or the Consortium? Or are you trying not to be?
  • Is your alignment ideological, practical, or personal - do you believe in the cause, or do you just need the paycheck?
  • Have you done something in service of your faction you're not proud of?
  • Is there someone on the other side you still respect, or care about?
  • If the war ends badly for your side, what do you do?

What Have You Lost?

  • Day Zero - were you near one of the 17 failing habitats? Did you lose someone?
  • The Rebreather Crisis changed most people's relationship to Earth. What did it change for you?
  • Have you ever had to choose between two things you valued equally, and what did you sacrifice?
  • Is there something you want back that you cannot have?
  • What is the thing you protect so hard that you're not honest about why?

How Do You Carry Yourself?

  • Titan-born bodies move differently. Are you adapted to low-g, and how does that show?
  • What does your suit look like? Is it standard-issue, modified, maintained obsessively, or barely functional?
  • Do you have visible body modifications - implants, markings, tattooed methane drops?
  • How do you speak to people from Earth? To people from other moons? Is that different?
  • What tells a careful observer that you've been through something hard?

What Do You Want?

  • In the immediate term: what are you trying to accomplish in the next few days?
  • In the medium term: what does a good outcome look like for you personally, regardless of what happens to the war?
  • What would you give up everything for?
  • What would you refuse to do, no matter who asked or what they offered?
  • What does the name Rhea mean to you - politically, personally, emotionally?
